# Chapter Four: Corsair

IN 1882, Pierpont was making half a million dollars a year, and the power balance within the Morgan empire began to tip from London to New York. To mark their new financial status, Pierpont and Fanny sold their high-stooped house on East Fortieth Street and bought a brownstone formerly owned by Isaac N. Phelps (of Phelps, Dodge copper fame) at 219 Madison Avenue at the northeast corner of Thirty-sixth Street, still in Manhattan’s Murray Hill neighborhood. In this less crowded New York, the East River was still visible from the house. At a time of sybaritic indulgence, when businessmen wallowed in luxury and showy greed was all the rage, the Morgan home was imposing but unadorned. Its entryway was flanked by Ionic columns, and a bay window overlooked Madison Avenue. Heavy wood furniture and bric-a-brac filled the rooms. In his high-ceilinged library, paneled in Santo Domin-gan mahogany, Pierpont set his massive desk; it stood in the middle of the room as if the library were the partners’ room of a merchant bank. This library was a place of such forbidding gloom that the staff of twelve servants called it the “black library.”[1](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2200547)
1882 年，皮尔庞特年收入已达五十万美元，摩根帝国权力天平开始从伦敦向纽约倾斜。为彰显全新金融地位，皮尔庞特与范妮卖掉了东四十街那栋高台阶宅邸，买下麦迪逊大道 219 号一栋褐石屋——这幢位于三十六街东北角、仍属曼哈顿默里山区域的房产，原属以铜业闻名的菲尔普斯-道奇公司创始人艾萨克·N·菲尔普斯。彼时纽约尚未拥挤不堪，从宅邸仍可望见东河风光。在骄奢淫逸之风盛行、商贾竞相沉溺奢华、贪婪炫耀成为潮流的年代，摩根府邸虽气派却不事雕饰。入口两侧立着爱奥尼柱式，凸窗正对麦迪逊大道；室内摆满厚重木家具与琳琅小摆设。 在他那间天花板高挑、以圣多明各桃花心木镶嵌墙壁的藏书室里，皮尔庞特摆下了他那张巨大的书桌；桌子立在房间正中，仿佛这藏书室就是一家商业银行的合伙人会议室。这间藏书室阴森得令人望而生畏，以至于十二名仆人都管它叫“黑屋”。” [1](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2200547)

A novel feature of the Morgan household was electricity: it was New York’s first electrically lighted private residence. Pierpont’s interest in the newly harnessed source of energy stemmed from a business deal: in 1878, Thomas Alva Edison had secured capital from the Morgan partners and other financiers to establish the Edison Electric Illuminating Company. Unfortunately, the infernal racket of the electrical generator was the bane of the Morgans’ neighbors. Downtown, Drexel, Morgan hosted early meetings of the Edison company and in 1882 became the first Wall Street office to draw electricity from Edison’s generating station at Pearl Street. Edison himself, in a Prince Albert coat, attended the debut of electric power at 23 Wall Street, and he kept his personal account at the bank.
摩根住宅的一大新奇之处在于电力：它是纽约第一座用电照明的私人住宅。皮尔庞特对这项新兴能源的兴趣源于一桩商业交易：1878 年，托马斯·阿尔瓦·爱迪生从摩根合伙人及其他金融家那里获得资金，成立了爱迪生电灯公司。不幸的是，发电机那恼人的噪音成了摩根家邻居们的噩梦。而在市区的德雷克塞尔-摩根公司，爱迪生公司早期的会议在此举办，1882 年，这里成为华尔街首个从爱迪生珍珠街发电站接电的办公室。爱迪生本人身穿燕尾服，亲临华尔街 23 号见证电力首次亮相，并将自己的个人账户设在这家银行。

The decision to stay in Murray Hill said much about the Morgans, who scorned the *nouveaux riches.* When they opted for that neighborhood, the “quality” were already moving uptown. Along Fifth Avenue, exhibitionist moguls built gaudy palaces, their styles plundered from European chateaus. From Fifty-first to Fifty-second streets, in elephantine splendor, rose William Henry Vanderbilt’s mansion. Between Fifty-seventh and Fifty-eighth streets, Cornelius Vanderbilt II, son of William Henry, built another palace on the present site of Bergdorf Goodman.
留在默里希尔的决定，充分说明了摩根家族对暴发户的不屑。当他们选择这片街区时，那些“上流人士”早已搬往城北。第五大道上，炫耀成性的巨头们建起浮夸的宫殿，风格全从欧洲城堡剽窃而来。第五十一街至五十二街之间，威廉·亨利·范德比尔特的豪宅拔地而起，气势磅礴。第五十七街至五十八街之间，他的儿子康奈利乌斯·范德比尔特二世又在如今波道夫·古德曼的旧址上造了另一座华宅。

Matthew Josephson has offered an unforgettable portrait of Gilded Age vulgarity:
马修·约瑟夫森曾留下一段令人难忘的描绘，尽显镀金时代的粗俗：

> At Delmonico’s the Silver, Gold and Diamond dinners of the socially prominent succeeded each other unfailingly. At one, each lady present, opening her napkin, found a gold bracelet with the monogram of the host. At another, cigarettes rolled in hundred-dollar bills were passed around after coffee and consumed with an authentic thrill. . . . One man gave a dinner to his dog, and presented him with a diamond collar worth \$ 15,000. At another dinner, costing \$20,000, each guest discovered in one of his oysters a magnificent black pearl. Another distracted individual longing for diversion had little holes bored into his teeth, into which a tooth expert inserted twin rows of diamonds; when he walked abroad his smile flashed and sparkled in the sunlight. . . . ”[2](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2200712)
> 在德尔莫尼科餐厅，社会名流的银宴、金宴和钻石宴接连不断。一场宴会上，每位在座女士打开餐巾时，都会发现一只刻有主人姓名缩写字母的金手镯。另一场宴会上，咖啡过后，人们传递着用百元美钞卷成的香烟，怀着真实的刺激将其点燃吞云吐雾……有位男士为自己的爱犬举办了一场晚宴，并赠予它一条价值 1.5 万美元的钻石项圈。另一场耗资 2 万美元的宴会上，每位宾客都在自己的牡蛎中发现了一颗华丽的黑珍珠。还有一位心神不宁、寻求刺激的富豪，在牙齿上钻出小孔，由牙科专家镶入两排钻石；当他外出漫步时，微笑便在阳光下闪烁生辉……

A cross between Connecticut Yankees and London aristocrats, the Morgans shrank from extravagance and shielded their lives from the newspapers. Like European *haute banque* families, the Morgans were very private. Pierpont was fanatic about his privacy and created an enduring image of a top-hatted tycoon snarling and brandishing a stick at photographers. He belonged to nineteen private clubs, most of the sort restricted to Anglo-Saxon Christian men, and liked to mingle with old money. Unlike most members, he preferred building clubs to using them. When some friends were blackballed from the Union Club, he had Stanford White design the Metropolitan Club, which acquired the tag of the Millionaire’s Club. Morgan was the first president. He was never a champion of social justice or equality. When Theodore Seligman, son of one of New York’s most prominent Jewish bankers, was blackballed from the Union League Club in 1893, Pierpont didn’t protest the exclusion.
康涅狄格州的扬基人和伦敦贵族的混血后代，摩根家族一向避免铺张，并竭力让自己的生活远离报纸的报道。如同欧洲的顶级银行家族，摩根家族极为注重隐私。皮尔庞特对自己的隐私近乎狂热，他塑造了一个戴着高顶礼帽的巨头形象，对着摄影师怒吼、挥舞手杖。他加入了十九家私人俱乐部，大多数只对盎格鲁-撒克逊裔的基督徒男性开放，并喜欢与旧钱阶层交往。与大多数会员不同，他更热衷于建造俱乐部而非使用它们。当几位朋友在联合俱乐部被投票拒绝入会时，他请斯坦福·怀特设计了“大都会俱乐部”，这个俱乐部后来有了“百万富翁俱乐部”的别称。摩根担任了首任主席。他从未成为社会正义或平等的倡导者。1893 年，纽约最著名的犹太银行家之子西奥多·塞利格曼在联盟俱乐部被拒绝入会时，皮尔庞特并未对这一排斥行为提出抗议。

For Pierpont, a gentleman wasn’t a rich man but a member of a social caste. He is associated with two statements about yachting that sum up his philosophy. The first is that “you can do business with anyone but you can only sail a boat with a gentleman,”[3](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2200874) and the second (perhaps apocryphal) that anyone who asked about the cost of maintaining a yacht shouldn’t buy one. He had no time for bounders or upstarts and despised the rich idle young men about town who pursued women in clubs and cafés. The Morgans would always be strong believers in the work ethic and the duties of the rich. They shunned the snobbish version of high society embodied by Mrs. Astor and Ward McAllister’s “Four Hundred”—supposedly the *crème de la crème* of New York society. In bluff, manly style, Pierpont would have thought their balls prissy or vulgar.
对皮尔庞特而言，绅士并非有钱人，而是特定社会阶层的一员。他的处世哲学凝结在两句关于游艇的名言中：其一是“你可以和任何人谈生意，但只能和绅士一起驾船出游”；其二（或许是杜撰的）则是“凡是问养一艘游艇要花多少钱的人，压根不该买游艇”。他看不上那些粗鄙暴发户，也鄙视在俱乐部和咖啡馆里追逐女人的城中游手好闲的富家子弟。摩根家族始终笃信劳动伦理与富人的责任，他们避开了由阿斯特夫人和沃德·麦卡利斯特的“四百人名单”——据说是纽约社交圈最精华的精英——所代表的势利上流社会。以皮尔庞特那种粗犷豪迈的男子气概，他大概会觉得那些舞会要么矫揉造作，要么低级庸俗。

A stuffed shirt, Pierpont liked to play chess or whist in the company of older, settled men. He believed in convention and always wore social uniforms suitable to the occasion—a bowler in winter, a Panama hat in summer, for instance. Even when he toured Egypt in 1877 he wore knickerbockers, watch chain, and pith helmet—the approved dress for the imperial tourist. “Physically and intellectually, Morgan reproduced the traditional old-time London banker,” said Alexander Dana Noyes.[4](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2201029) At the office, sitting at his rolltop desk, he wore stiff winged collars, ascots, and heavily starched shirts—trademarks of the serious banker. Only on sweltering days would he peel off his coat in the clublike atmosphere. Like his father, he called himself a merchant and his firm a countinghouse.
一个自命不凡的家伙，皮尔庞特喜欢和那些上了年纪、稳重的男人下棋或打惠斯特牌。他信奉传统，总是穿着符合场合的社交制服——冬天戴圆顶礼帽，夏天戴巴拿马草帽。就连 1877 年游历埃及时，他也穿着灯笼裤、挂着表链、戴着遮阳帽——标准的帝国游客打扮。“无论从外形还是气质上，摩根都复刻了传统的旧式伦敦银行家，”亚历山大·达纳·诺伊斯这样评价道。 [4](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2201029) 在办公室里，他坐在卷盖式书桌前，穿着硬挺的翼领衬衫、阿斯科特领带和浆得笔挺的衬衣——这些都是严肃银行家的标志。只有在闷热难耐的日子，他才会在俱乐部般的氛围中脱下外套。像他父亲一样，他称自己为商人，称他的公司为账房。

The early 1880s saw Pierpont’s metamorphosis from a dashing, muscular young man into the portly tycoon with fierce visage and blown-up nose. Now in his forties, he had graying hair and eyebrows and still sported a handlebar mustache. The acne rosacea that had troubled him since adolescence took root in his nose, enlarging and inflaming it until it became Wall Street’s most talked-about protuberance. Over the years, it would take on a cauliflower texture. Many people would notice a link between the nose and Pierpont’s fiery temper. The nose certainly contributed to an insecurity and lack of social ease that were thinly masked by a barking voice and tyrannical manner. The blustery tone warned the world not to stare at the face. The nose must have been a terrible handicap for a shy, self-conscious man with a tremendous need for female admiration.
19 世纪 80 年代初，皮尔庞特从一名英俊健壮的年轻男子蜕变为面容凶狠、鼻子红肿的胖大亨。年过四旬的他头发与眉毛已渐花白，依然留着翘八字胡。自青春期起困扰他的酒渣鼻在鼻子上扎根，不断肿大发炎，最终成为华尔街最热议的"突起物"。随着岁月流逝，这鼻子逐渐呈现出菜花般的质地。许多人注意到这只鼻子与皮尔庞特火爆脾气之间的关联。这鼻子无疑加剧了他的不安与社交局促，只不过被粗声粗气的嗓门和专横的态度勉强遮掩。那虚张声势的语调仿佛在警告世人：别盯着这张脸看。对于一个既渴望女性仰慕、又羞怯敏感的男人而言，这只鼻子想必是可怕的缺陷。

The body swelled with the face. In the 1880s, a generation of Wall Street bankers was doomed by the wisdom of one William Evarts, who credited his longevity to “never under any circumstance having taken exercise. ”[5](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2201193) Pierpont usually played cards at a club after work rather than join in a game of tennis. He occasionally lifted dumbbells, but in the late 1880s a medical sage advised him to “stop exercise in every form. Never even walk when you can take a cab.”[6](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2201326) Pierpont loyally followed doctor’s orders, doing so while smoking Havana cigars so big and black that they were dubbed Hercules’ clubs.[7](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2201515) A teetotaler by day—the Morgan banks, by tradition, never served alcohol at lunch—he compensated for this abstinence at night, progressing from predinner cocktails to sherry or claret with meals and then to brandy or port afterward. More than husky, he began to develop the sleek girth that symbolized contemporary tycoons.
身体和脸一起膨胀了。19 世纪 80 年代，一代华尔街银行家因威廉·埃瓦茨的智慧而注定失败。此人将自己的长寿归功于“绝不在任何情况下锻炼身体”。 [5](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2201193) 皮尔庞特下班后通常在俱乐部打牌，而不是去打网球。他偶尔会举举哑铃，但在 80 年代末，一位医学权威建议他“停止一切形式的锻炼。能坐马车就别走路。” [6](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2201326) 皮尔庞特忠实地遵从医嘱，一边照做，一边抽着又大又黑的哈瓦那雪茄，这些雪茄被戏称为“大力神棒”。 [7](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2201515) 白天他是滴酒不沾的禁酒主义者——摩根银行按传统从不提供午餐酒水——但到了晚上，他会补上这份节制：从餐前鸡尾酒开始，到用餐时的雪利酒或红葡萄酒，再到餐后的白兰地或波特酒。他不再只是强壮，而是开始长出了象征当时大亨的圆润腰围。

Although a retiring person beneath his bossy manner, Pierpont maintained an acquaintance with an extensive number of people. As a merchant banker, he had to cultivate clients, and his business life was necessarily social. As a later Baring Brothers chairman remarked of the business, “One of the facets of the art is that if you do not get on with the people you are trying to advise, then you find yourselves out the door.”[8](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2201670) And Pierpont engaged in a constant whirl of dinners and civic functions.
尽管皮尔庞特在专横的外表下其实是个害羞的人，但他与大量的人保持着交情。作为商人银行家，他必须培养客户关系，他的商业生活必然具有社交性。正如后来一位巴林兄弟公司的主席在谈到这一行业时所说：“这门艺术的一个方面是，如果你与你试图提供建议的人相处不来，那么你就会发现自己被扫地出门。” [8](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2201670) 皮尔庞特则不断地投身于宴会和公民活动的旋涡之中。

These social pressures took their toll on his marriage, which had already begun to turn into a cold, empty charade. Fanny Morgan was bashful and lacked all relish for the social duties incumbent upon a merchant banker’s wife. Sad and anxious, sweet and pious, she preferred reading, gossiping with friends, talking about religion, and discussing social questions. She would be more popular with both their children and their grandchildren than would the dagger-eyed Pierpont. As his world grew larger, Fanny’s spirit was either not large enough or not willing enough to fill that space with him. One also suspects that the couple clashed as a result of their very similarity. Both were sensitive and high-strung and too melancholic to provide much solace for the other. Fanny wasn’t a tonic to Pierpont’s habitual moodiness, and he was doubtless much too busy to attend to her needs. The practical marriage, the supposed antidote to the Mimi affair, turned out to be dangerously impractical.
这些社会压力让他的婚姻不堪重负，这段婚姻早已沦为一场冰冷而空洞的伪装。范妮·摩根生性羞怯，对身为银行家妻子所需承担的社交职责毫无兴致。她忧伤焦虑、温柔虔诚，更喜欢读书、与朋友闲聊、谈论宗教和社会问题。比起目光如刀的皮尔庞特，她显然更受子孙后代的喜爱。随着皮尔庞特的世界日益扩张，范妮的心胸要么不够宽广，要么不愿与他一同去填满那片天地。人们也不禁猜测，这对夫妇过于相似，反而导致了矛盾。两人都敏感而易怒，又都过于忧郁，无法给予对方太多慰藉。范妮无法缓解皮尔庞特惯常的阴郁情绪，而他无疑也过于忙碌，无暇顾及她的需求。这场本应成为米米事件解药的实用婚姻，到头来却危险地不切实际。

When Junius returned to London after his 1877 dinner, Pierpont followed. It was the first Christmas he spent away from his children. The next year, Fanny didn’t join him for the annual spring trip abroad, and he thereafter developed the habit of traveling to Europe with one of his daughters, spending months apart from his wife each year. These trips combined business and pleasure, and provided cover for infidelity. As a high Victorian, he was proper and respectful toward Fanny in public, even as their separations lengthened. Over time, she would become morose and something of an invalid, pouring her heart out, to her son Jack, among others.
1877 年的那场晚宴后，Junius 回到伦敦，Pierpont 也紧随其后。那是他第一次没和孩子们一起过圣诞节。次年，Fanny 没有陪他参加每年春天的海外之旅，此后他便养成了带一个女儿去欧洲旅行的习惯，每年都要和妻子分开好几个月。这些旅行公私兼顾，也为他的不忠提供了掩护。作为维多利亚时代的上流绅士，他在公开场合对 Fanny 依然得体尊重，尽管两人分居的时间越来越长。久而久之，她变得郁郁寡欢，体弱多病，常常向儿子 Jack 等人倾诉心声。

Pierpont wasn’t the sort to suffer a loveless marriage lightly. As revealed by his love for Mimi, he was highly romantic. He made pilgrimages to Mimi’s grave in Fairfield, Connecticut, traveling there on the anniversary of their wedding or of her death.[9](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2201936) His eyes cloudy and troubled, he had the soul of a voluptuary beneath a banker’s custom-made suit. Even as he scared people away, he was a lonely man, carrying around a vast despair that he couldn’t share with anyone. His unhappy marriage probably plunged him deeper into business while also denying him the pleasure of his triumphs.
皮尔庞特并非那种能轻易忍受无爱婚姻的人。从他对咪咪的爱恋中便可看出，他极为浪漫。他会年年前往康涅狄格州费尔菲尔德的咪咪墓前朝圣，或是在他们的结婚纪念日，或是在她的忌日。 [9](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2201936) 他眼神迷离而忧郁，在银行家定制西装的包裹下，藏着一颗耽于享乐的灵魂。尽管他总把人吓跑，内心却无比孤独，怀揣着无法向任何人倾诉的巨大绝望。那段不幸的婚姻或许让他更深地埋头于事业，同时也剥夺了他享受成功的乐趣。

PIERPONT’S connections in the realm of charity were almost as extensive as his business interests. He preferred to give to religious, cultural, and educational causes, not to social welfare agencies. He never tried to solve the problem of poverty. He wanted to build institutions that were private and elite. He was an original patron of the Metropolitan Museum of Art and the American Museum of Natural History, had a box within the Metropolitan Opera’s Golden Horseshoe (he liked romantic, florid operas, especially *Il Trovatore)*, and was a major contributor to Saint Luke’s Hospital. After Junius took in S. Endicott Pea-body (a distant relative of George’s) as a partner in London, Pierpont helped his son, the Reverend Endicott Peabody, to buy ninety acres north of Boston for a new prep school, Groton. Modeled after Rugby, it was supposed to develop a good, manly, Christian character in its pupils. Ironically, it spawned that arch enemy of the House of Morgan—Franklin Delano Roosevelt.
皮尔庞特在慈善界的人脉几乎与他的商业利益一样广泛。他更倾向于捐助宗教、文化和教育事业，而非社会福利机构。他从未试图解决贫困问题，而是想建立私密且精英化的机构。他是大都会艺术博物馆和美国自然历史博物馆的早期赞助人，在大都会歌剧院的"金马蹄"包厢拥有专座（他偏爱浪漫华丽的歌剧，尤其是《游吟诗人》），并曾为圣卢克医院慷慨解囊。朱尼厄斯在伦敦将 S·恩迪科特·皮博迪（乔治的远房亲戚）纳为合伙人后，皮尔庞特帮助其子——牧师恩迪科特·皮博迪——在波士顿北部购买了九十英亩土地，用于创办一所新的预科学校——格罗顿学校。这所以拉格比公学为蓝本的学校，旨在培养学生善良、刚毅的基督教品格。讽刺的是，它却孕育了摩根家族的宿敌——富兰克林·德拉诺·罗斯福。

Through his friend and personal physician, Dr. James W. Markoe, Pierpont gave one of his rare gifts to the immigrant masses then streaming into New York’s Lower East Side. In 1893, Markoe told him of an operation he had performed in a tenement kitchen to save an immigrant mother and her baby. Pierpont counted out three hundred-dollar bills. “See that she gets the proper care,” he said, handing the money to the doctor.[10](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2202094) Eventually Dr. Markoe persuaded him to contribute over \$1 million to erect a new building for the New York Lying-in Hospital, where nurses would provide poor pregnant women with food, milk, and prenatal care. Dr. Markoe became the director. As Pierpont became more of a philanderer, his concern for unwed mothers would be the subject of wisecracks about town, as well as stories of doctors at the hospital who married Pierpont’s mistresses.
通过他的朋友兼私人医生詹姆斯·W·马科博士，皮尔庞特罕见地向当时涌入纽约下东区的移民群体伸出了援手。1893 年，马科博士告诉他，自己在某间廉租房厨房里进行了一场手术，拯救了一位移民母亲和她的新生儿。皮尔庞特数出三张百元大钞，把钱递给医生，说道：“务必让她得到妥善照料。” [10](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2202094) 后来，马科博士说服他捐资超过一百万美元，为纽约产科医院建造新楼。在那里，护士们会为贫困孕妇提供食物、牛奶和产前护理。马科博士担任院长。随着皮尔庞特风流韵事渐多，他对未婚母亲的关怀成了城里人茶余饭后的笑料，更有传言称，医院里有些医生最终娶了他的情妇。

But the institution that most absorbed Pierpont was the Episcopal church, which was part of the Anglican Communion. Religion united his values—beauty, order, hierarchical relationships, veneration of the past, pageantry and pomp. As New York’s most influential Episcopal layman, he attended the church’s triennial conventions and participated in its abstruse debates. Religion logically accompanied the moralism that drove him at work and lay at the bottom of his indignation at American business practices. His maternal grandfather was a preacher, his paternal grandfather a lusty hymn-singer, and his father’s banking maxims were phrased in the epigrammatic style of sermons, Junius often sounded like a frustrated clergyman: “Self-approbation and a feeling that God approves will bring a far greater happiness than all the wealth the world can give.”[11](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2202276) And Pierpont himself was wont to pontificate at 23 Wall Street.
但最让皮尔庞特投入的机构是圣公会，它属于圣公宗的一部分。宗教将他的价值观——美感、秩序、等级关系、对过去的崇敬、盛典与排场——融为一体。作为纽约最具影响力的圣公会平信徒，他出席该教会每三年一次的大会，并参与其深奥的辩论。宗教自然而然地与驱动他工作的道德主义相伴而行，也是他对美国商业惯例感到愤慨的根源。他的外祖父是牧师，祖父是一位热衷唱赞美诗的人，而他父亲关于银行业务的箴言则以布道式的警句风格表达出来，朱尼厄斯常常听起来像个失意的牧师：“自我认可和感受到上帝的认可所带来的幸福，远胜于世上的所有财富。” [11](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2202276) 而皮尔庞特本人也惯常在华尔街 23 号高谈阔论。

For Pierpont and Fanny, Sundays were devoted to religion. They attended Saint George’s Church on Stuyvesant Square, where Pierpont had been a vestryman since 1868, and spent Sunday evenings singing hymns. To gratify Fanny, Pierpont also attended Wednesday evening sessions of the Mendelssohn Club, a choral group. In his early years, he had a strongly prudish streak. In general, his religious interests weren’t tied to codes of earthly conduct. Religion moved him on a more primitive level. Whether roaring out hymns at revival meetings or sitting alone in Saint George’s, savoring organ music in semidarkness, he seemed mesmerized by ritual and lapsed into reveries of mystic depth.
对皮尔庞特和范妮来说，周日是奉献给宗教的日子。他们去斯图文森特广场的圣乔治教堂做礼拜，皮尔庞特自 1868 年起就担任那里的教区委员，周日晚上则一起唱赞美诗。为了让范妮开心，皮尔庞特也会参加周三晚上的门德尔松合唱团活动。早年他有着强烈的清教徒倾向。总体而言，他对宗教的兴趣与世俗行为准则无关，宗教在更原始的层面触动着他。无论是在复兴集会上高唱赞美诗，还是独自坐在圣乔治教堂里，在昏暗的光线中品味管风琴音乐，他似乎都被仪式深深吸引，陷入神秘的冥想之中。

Approaching Scripture with the literalism of a fundamentalist, Pierpont was as credulous as a child. In 1882, he visited Palestine. Deeply moved, he wrote Fanny about the sensations he experienced before the doorway of Christ’s sepulcher: “There is the slab on which He was laid. Impelled by an impulse impossible to resist you fall on your knees before that shrine.”[12](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2202431) In later years, he told his librarian, Belle da Costa Greene, that he believed every word in the Bible, including the account of Jonah and the whale. Once traveling down the Nile with Bishop William Lawrence, he pointed out the precise spot where Moses was plucked from the bulrushes and insisted it happened exactly as set forth in the Bible. In view of this credulity, it is not surprising that Pierpont was fascinated by the occult. For years, he commissioned the astrologer Evangeline Adams to read his horoscope, asking her to study his stars on everything from politics to the stock market. When his son, lack, was born, the infant’s horoscope showed a cardinal cross, associated with depressions—an apt prophecy for the Morgan who steered the bank through 1929.
以原教旨主义者的字面主义态度对待圣经，皮尔庞特天真得像个孩子。1882 年，他访问巴勒斯坦。深受触动之下，他写信给范妮，描述自己在基督墓穴门口感受到的震撼：“那就是他躺过的石板。被一股无法抗拒的冲动驱使，你会在那座圣殿前跪下来。” [12](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2202431) 晚年时，他告诉自己的图书管理员贝尔·达·科斯塔·格林，他相信圣经里的每一个字，包括约拿和大鱼的故事。一次与威廉·劳伦斯主教沿尼罗河旅行时，他指出了摩西从蒲草箱中被救起的精确地点，并坚称这一切正如圣经所记载的那样发生。鉴于这种轻信，皮尔庞特对神秘事物着迷也就不足为奇了。多年来，他委托占星家伊万杰琳·亚当斯解读他的星盘，请她研究他的星象，涉及从政治到股市的一切。 他的儿子拉克出生时，星盘上显示了一个主十字相——这一格局常与抑郁、低迷联系在一起。这仿佛是对那位带领银行挺过 1929 年华尔街大崩盘的摩根的恰当预言。

In 1883, the thirty-three-year-old Rev. William S. Rainsford took over as Saint George’s rector. He was a handsome young Irishman with a Cambridge education. Having bankrolled the church’s activity, Pierpont had a hand in his appointment. As a social reformer and fiery exponent of the “social gospel,” Rainsford told Morgan he would take the job only if the church were democratic and open to the poor. “Done,” said Morgan, who agreed to make up the church’s deficits.[13](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2202608) And Rainsford indeed welcomed the poor into Saint George’s now-free pews. Eventually the two men became so close that they had breakfast together every Monday morning at 219 Madison Avenue, and Morgan built several new church buildings.
 1883 年，年仅 33 岁的威廉·S·雷恩斯福德牧师接掌圣乔治教堂的教区长一职。这位英俊的爱尔兰青年毕业于剑桥大学。由于摩根曾资助教堂的事务，他在雷恩斯福德的任命中插了一手。作为一名社会改革家与“社会福音”的热忱倡导者，雷恩斯福德向摩根提出条件：只有教堂实行民主并向穷人开放，他才肯接下这份差事。“成交，”摩根爽快答应，并同意填补教堂的赤字。 [13](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2202608) 而雷恩斯福德也确实打开了圣乔治教堂如今免费的长椅，迎接穷人的到来。两人最终亲密无间，每周一上午都会在麦迪逊大道 219 号共进早餐，摩根还为教堂新建了好几栋楼。

Dr. Rainsford later ran into trouble when he tried to enlarge and democratize the vestry, which met in Morgan’s “black library.” This went against the grain of Pierpont’s arm’s-length philanthropy, and he bluntly retorted: “I do not want the vestry democratized. I want it to remain a body of gentlemen whom I can ask to meet me in my study—gentlemen who would feel at home and who could make up deficits out of their pockets.”[14](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2202789) He sent a letter to Rainsford, resigning his post as senior warden; the young rector stubbornly refused to accept it. For several weeks, the two men continued their Monday breakfasts, both eating in silence. During these meals, Pierpont may have recalled the rich men who hounded his reformer grandfather, the Reverend Pierpont. After several weeks of this standoff, Morgan invited Rainsford to see him set sail for Europe. Alone with Rainsford in his stateroom, Pierpont threw his arms around him and exclaimed, “Rainsford, pray for me, pray for me.”[15](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2202923) The feud ended with this melodramatic display of contrition.
后来，雷恩斯福德博士试图扩大并民主化教区委员会时，遇到了麻烦——这个委员会在摩根的“黑色书房”里开会。这与皮尔庞特保持距离的慈善理念格格不入，他直截了当地反驳道：“我不希望委员会民主化。我希望它仍是一群我能请到书房里来的绅士——这些绅士不仅感觉自在，还能自掏腰包填补亏空。” [14](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2202789) 他给雷恩斯福德写了一封信，辞去了资深教区委员的职务；这位年轻的教区长固执地拒绝接受。连续几周，两人仍继续周一的早餐之约，但全程沉默不语。用餐时，皮尔庞特或许想起了当年那些纠缠他那身为改革者的祖父——牧师皮尔庞特的富人们。僵持数周后，摩根邀请雷恩斯福德前来送他启航前往欧洲。在特等客舱里与雷恩斯福德独处时，皮尔庞特突然张开双臂抱住他，喊道：“雷恩斯福德，为我祈祷，为我祈祷吧。””这场世仇以如此戏剧化的悔罪表演告终。

Rainsford has left interesting impressions of Pierpont’s religious faith: “His beliefs were to him precious heirlooms. He bowed before them as the Russian bows to the ’ikon’ before he salutes the master of the house.”[16](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2203057) He saw that for Pierpont the Church wasn’t an active, reforming spirit, but a repository of ancient beauty, powerful because it was archaic and unchanging. Rainsford also credited Pierpont with intense loyalty and forthright honesty: “When he said a thing, and looked full at you as he said it, to doubt him was impossible.”[17](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2203212) It was the same look that transfixed two generations of railroad presidents and industrial moguls.
雷恩斯福德对皮尔庞特的宗教信仰留下了有趣的印象：“他的信仰对他而言是珍贵的传家宝。他在向主人致敬之前，先像俄国人对着圣像鞠躬一样，在信仰面前低头。” [16](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2203057) 他看到，对皮尔庞特来说，教会并非积极革新的精神力量，而是古老美的宝库，因其古老与不变而强大。雷恩斯福德还赞赏皮尔庞特极度的忠诚与坦率：“当他说出一句话，并直视着你说出时，怀疑他是不可能的。” [17](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2203212) 正是这种目光，曾让两代铁路总裁和实业巨头们动弹不得。

ALTHOUGH the business life of Pierpont Morgan was bound up with the railroads, Pierpont felt more keenly the allure of the sea. At a time when private railroad cars were common showpieces among tycoons, Pierpont never owned one and took private cars, as needed, from the railroads he directed. By midlife, the sea was his best remedy for depression, the place where he escaped from the perpetual strain of the office and was liberated from care. When a yacht-owning fad swept fashionable New York in the 1880s, he needed little inducement to participate. In 1882, he bought the first of a series of enormous yachts, named *Corsair*, and joined the New York Yacht Club. This black-hulled steam yacht—165 feet long and the second largest in the club’s fleet—marked a new Morgan magnificence.
尽管皮尔庞特·摩根的商业生涯与铁路紧密相连，但他内心更向往大海。在那个铁路巨头们争相炫耀私人车厢的年代，摩根从未拥有过一辆，而是根据需要临时调遣自己旗下铁路公司的车厢。到了中年，大海成了他纾解忧郁的良药——在那里，他能暂时远离办公室的永无止境的压力，卸下一切烦恼。19 世纪 80 年代，当拥有一艘游艇的风潮席卷纽约上流社会时，他几乎没费什么劲就被吸引了。1882 年，他买下了一系列巨型游艇中的第一艘，命名为"Corsair"，并加入了纽约游艇俱乐部。这艘黑色船身的蒸汽游艇长达 165 英尺，在俱乐部舰队中排名第二，标志着摩根家族的新一轮豪华风范。

It was probably no coincidence that Pierpont bought the *Corsair* soon after it first became apparent that his marriage was disintegrating. The boat was more than a showy bauble. It gave him a social setting beyond Fanny and the children and would later figure in many stories of secret revelry. It permitted an outlaw life beyond the stuffy Victorian bounds of his early married days. He created a group of friends known as the Corsair Club, which provided the camouflage needed to smuggle women on board. The ship was also a second home, particularly when Fanny and the children retreated up the Hudson to Cragston for the summer. Often, Pierpont would dine on the ship and spend the night as it lay at anchor off Manhattan.
皮尔庞特买下“海盗号”很可能并非巧合，几乎就在他的婚姻开始显现裂痕之后不久。这艘船远不止是一件招摇的玩物。它为他提供了一个超越范妮和孩子们的社交空间，后来更成为许多秘密狂欢故事的舞台。它让他得以过上一种无法无天的生活，挣脱早婚岁月里那令人窒息的维多利亚时代束缚。他组建了一个名为“海盗俱乐部”的朋友圈子，为偷偷带女人上船提供了所需的掩护。这艘船也是他的第二个家，尤其是当范妮和孩子们夏天北上哈德逊河畔的克拉格斯顿避暑时。皮尔庞特常常在停泊于曼哈顿近海的船上用餐并过夜。

Purchase of the *Corsair* coincided with a new phase in Pierpont’s career, in which he became an arbiter as well as a financier of railroads. The boat was useful as a meeting place to settle disputes, a secret clubhouse beyond spying eyes. Pierpont had an actor’s talent for creating dramatic backdrops for his exploits, and the *Corsair* allowed his business life to take on an aura of operatic flamboyance. This was never truer than in the 1885 dispute between the Pennsylvania Railroad and the New York Central over a railroad called the West Shore.
购入“海盗号”游艇时，皮尔庞特的职业生涯正迈入新阶段——他不仅成为铁路界的金融家，更化身仲裁者。这艘船成了解决争端的绝密会所，一个远离窥探的秘密俱乐部。皮尔庞特天生具备演员般的禀赋，擅于为自己的行动营造戏剧性舞台背景，而“海盗号”更让他的商业生涯平添了歌剧般的华丽色彩。这一点在 1885 年宾夕法尼亚铁路公司与纽约中央铁路公司争夺西岸铁路控制权的纠纷中表现得淋漓尽致。

Pierpont’s involvement had a personal dimension. One day in 1881, he saw a peddler leading a pair of donkeys up Broad Street; delighted by their resemblance to small donkeys he had seen in Egypt, he sent a clerk out to buy them. Christened Beelzebub and Apollyon, they were favorites of the Morgan children at Cragston. The following year, his children felt menaced by Irish ruffians building a new railroad below his house on the Hudson’s west bank, and Pierpont forbade them to ride unaccompanied by an adult. At the same time, blasts of explosives from the construction of this new West Shore road rattled Cragston’s windows, invading the tranquil Morgan hideaway.
皮尔庞特的介入带有个人色彩。1881 年的一天，他看到一个货郎牵着两头驴子沿宽街走来，这两头驴让他想起了在埃及见过的那些矮小驴子，顿感欣喜，便派了个职员出去买下它们。这两头驴被命名为别西卜和阿波利翁，成为孩子们在克拉格斯顿的最爱。次年，他的孩子们感觉受到了一群爱尔兰恶棍的威胁——这些恶棍正在他哈德逊河西岸宅邸下方修建一条新铁路，皮尔庞特因而禁止孩子们在没有成人陪伴的情况下骑马。与此同时，这条新建的西岸铁路施工时的爆破声震得克拉格斯顿的窗户咯咯作响，扰乱了摩根家这片宁静的避世之所。

The West Shore was that railroad bane of the period—the blackmail line. Extortion artists would lay down parallel lines just to be bought out by an established road. Since railroads were natural monopolies and couldn’t survive much direct competition, they could be easily threatened by small competitors. The West Shore ran up the west side of the Hudson, parallel to the New York Central on the opposite bank, then tracked the Central to Buffalo. It was widely believed the powerful Pennsylvania Railroad stood behind the West Shore. So in retaliation, the New York Central broke ground on a South Pennsylvania road to compete with the Pennsylvania from Philadelphia to Pittsburgh.
西岸铁路就是那个时代铁路业的祸根——一条勒索线。敲诈者铺设平行的线路，只为了被已有铁路公司收购。由于铁路是天然垄断行业，难以承受直接竞争，很容易受到小型竞争对手的威胁。西岸铁路沿着哈得孙河西岸铺设，与对岸的纽约中央铁路平行，然后顺着中央铁路的路线一直延伸到布法罗。人们普遍认为，强大的宾夕法尼亚铁路是西岸铁路背后的支持者。作为报复，纽约中央铁路在南宾夕法尼亚破土动工，修建一条与宾夕法尼亚铁路从费城到匹兹堡竞争的线路。

A fierce rate war between the West Shore and the New York Central hammered down stock and bond prices for both companies, confirming Pierpont’s growing hatred of competition. It came at a precarious time for railroad bankers. During a stock market plunge in 1883, there was a near-panic in American rail stocks in London, producing a rising clamor for a financial czar who could arbitrarily settle such disputes. Cyrus Field cabled Junius, “Many of our business men seem to have lost their heads. What we want is some cool-headed strong man to lead.”[18](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2203338) As fiscal agent for the road, Junius watched with alarm as New York Central stock fell below par for the first time; its dividend was halved. In early 1885, Pierpont went to London to consult with Junius and fumed over the “absurd struggle for preeminence” plunging America’s railroads into internecine warfare.[19](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2203501) By the spring of 1885, the West Shore had gone into the hands of a receiver, while the hard-pressed New York Central deferred critical maintenance.
西岸铁路与纽约中央铁路之间激烈的运费战重创了两家公司的股票和债券价格，这也进一步印证了皮尔庞特对竞争日益加深的憎恶。这场争斗恰逢铁路银行家们处境岌岌可危的时刻。1883 年股市暴跌期间，伦敦市场的美国铁路股几乎陷入恐慌，要求设立一位能随意裁决此类纠纷的金融沙皇的呼声日益高涨。赛勒斯·菲尔德电告朱尼厄斯：“我们许多商人似乎已失去理智。我们需要的是一位冷静果断的强人来掌舵。” [18](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2203338) 作为该线路的财务代理人，朱尼厄斯惊恐地看到纽约中央铁路的股价首次跌破面值，股息也被腰斩。1885 年初，皮尔庞特前往伦敦与朱尼厄斯商议，对这场让美国铁路陷入内斗的“荒谬的争霸战”愤懑不已。 到 1885 年春天，West Shore 已落入破产管理人之手，而捉襟见肘的纽约中央铁路（New York Central）则一再推迟关键维护工作。

It seems anomalous that America’s most famous financier was a sworn foe of free markets. Yet it followed logically from the anarchy of late nineteenth-century railroads, with their rate wars, blackmail, lines, and lack of standardized gauges. To destroy competing lines, railroads could simply refuse to transfer freight to roads that abutted theirs. From an engineering standpoint, Pierpont knew little about railroads. What he did know was that they required steady revenues to cover their fixed interest costs on bonds marketed in New York and London. In the mid-1880s, freight rates were declining sharply under the pressure of savage price-cutting. Pierpont decided that “the principal thing was to secure a harmony between the Pennsylvania and New York Central.”[20](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2203656)
美国最著名的金融家竟是自由市场的死敌，这看似反常，却与 19 世纪末铁路行业的混乱局面——运费战、敲诈勒索、线路乱象、缺乏标准化轨距——一脉相承。为了摧毁竞争线路，铁路公司可以干脆拒绝为相邻线路转运货物。从工程角度看，Pierpont 对铁路所知甚少。他真正明白的是，铁路需要稳定的收入来覆盖在纽约和伦敦发行的债券的固定利息成本。19 世纪 80 年代中期，在残酷杀价压力下，运费急剧下降。Pierpont 认定，“首要之事是确保宾夕法尼亚铁路与纽约中央铁路之间达成共识。”

On the sultry morning of July 20, 1885, with an impresario’s flair, Pierpont staged a reconciliation between America’s two largest railroads. After picking up the New York Central’s president, Chauncey Depew, he crossed to a New Jersey pier and took aboard George H. Roberts, president, and Frank Thomson, vice-president, of the Pennsylvania Railroad. Pierpont always denied his yacht was chosen for the sake of secrecy. “I do not know that that was a part of the consideration,” he later testified. “It might have been.”[21](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2203843)
1885 年 7 月 20 日那个闷热的早晨，皮尔庞特像个老练的演出经理人一样，安排了一场美国两大铁路公司的和解。他接上纽约中央铁路公司总裁昌西·迪普后，又前往新泽西的一个码头，迎上了宾夕法尼亚铁路公司总裁乔治·H·罗伯茨和副总裁弗兰克·汤姆森。皮尔庞特始终否认选择他的游艇是为了保密。"我不知道那是不是考虑的一部分，"他后来作证说。"也许有吧。" [21](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2203843)

Before bringing both parties on board, he worked out the broad outlines of a truce. While the *Corsair* sailed up and down the Hudson, he sat under the rear awning, flanked by the railroad chiefs and smoking his nightmarishly huge black cigar. He stressed the displeasure of European investors with American railroads, but mostly let the railway men debate among themselves. In general, he used two negotiating ploys. He would create a “no-exit” situation and add to it threats that his rivals faced a deadline—a way of building tension and softening up the parties. Also, by saying little, he underscored his position as honest broker and permitted the antagonists to vent their anger. Pierpont was, by nature, a laconic man. He had no gift for sustained analysis; his genius was in the brief, sudden brainstorm. As one lawyer said of him, “Morgan has one chief mental asset—a tremendous five minutes’ concentration of thought.”[22](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2203978) By the time the railroad presidents were deposited on their respective shores at seven o’clock that evening, they had agreed to buy out each other’s lines and desist from their mutually destructive warfare. Years later, the tunnels and embankments from the abandoned South Pennsylvania line would be incorporated into the Pennsylvania Turnpike. And as the New York Central’s business expanded, it enlisted the West Shore tracks for a second line along the Hudson River.
在促成双方坐上谈判桌前，他已草拟出休战的大致框架。当"海盗号"游艇沿着哈德逊河上下航行时，他坐在船尾遮阳篷下，两边坐着铁路巨头们，嘴里叼着那根大得吓人的黑雪茄。他屡次提及欧洲投资者对美国铁路的不满，但大部分时间都任由铁路大亨们自行争论。总的来说，他使用了两种谈判策略：制造一种"无法退出的局面"，并加上对手面临最后期限的威胁——以此制造紧张气氛，让对方松口。同时，他少言寡语，强调自己作为诚实中间人的角色，让对手发泄怒气。皮尔庞特天生话少，不擅长持续性的分析；他的天才在于短暂而突然的灵感迸发。正如一位律师对他的评价："摩根最突出的心智资产——就是那惊人的五分钟高度集中的思考。"”当晚七点，铁路大亨们各自回到出发地时，已达成协议：互相收购对方的线路，停止这场两败俱伤的恶性竞争。多年后，废弃的南宾夕法尼亚铁路隧道和路基被改建为宾夕法尼亚收费公路。而随着纽约中央铁路业务扩张，它又将西岸铁路的轨道纳入麾下，作为哈德逊河沿岸的第二条铁路线。

The newspapers lionized the author of this Great Railroad Treaty of 1885, also known as the Corsair Compact.[23](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2204168) Pierpont had pulled off such a masterly feat that even Junius—so stingy with compliments—told Fanny, “Pierpont handled the West Shore affair better than I could have done it myself.”[24](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2204332) Pierpont was forty-eight when Junius voiced this unprecedented compliment. Once again, Pierpont had performed the kind of task of industrial arbitration that would later be left to courts and public commissions. In the rough-and-tumble of the Baronial Age, competition was naked and brutal, and businessmen lacked trade groups in which they could discuss common problems. Bankers could intervene as neutral parties, particularly where, as with Drexel, Morgan, they had performed work for both companies. Over the years, Pierpont would employ the sharpest lawyers, yet his preferred style was more British—informal deals, handshakes over brandy and cigars, cordial clubroom chats among bankers as they stood in frock coats and stiff collars. The Morgans were never litigious. During one railroad battle, Junius wrote Pierpont, “I hope you will not be tempted into litigation. Life is too short for that.”[25](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2204488)
报纸对这位 1885 年《大铁路条约》（又称《海盗契约》）的作者极尽赞美之词。 [23](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2204168) 皮尔庞特完成了一项如此精湛的壮举，连一向吝于赞美的朱尼厄斯都对范妮说：“西肖尔事件的处理，皮尔庞特做得比我自己来还要好。” [24](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2204332) 当朱尼厄斯说出这番前所未有的赞词时，皮尔庞特已经四十八岁了。他再次履行了那种后来交由法院和公共委员会处理的产业仲裁职责。在“男爵时代”的混战中，竞争是赤裸而残酷的，商人们缺乏可以共同商讨问题的行业组织。银行家能以中立方的身份介入，尤其是在像德雷克塞尔、摩根这样为两家公司都提供过服务的情况下。 多年来，皮尔庞特身边不乏顶尖律师，但他更偏爱英式做派——非正式的交易，在白兰地和雪茄之间握手成交，银行家们穿着礼服大衣、立着硬领，在俱乐部里亲切交谈。摩根家族从不轻易诉诸法律。在一场铁路争夺战中，朱尼厄斯写信给皮尔庞特说：“我希望你不要被牵扯进诉讼。人生苦短，不值得为这种事耗费精力。”” [25](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2204488)

Bloodletting among railroads intensified in the 1880s. Several rail roads skirted bankruptcy. In 1886, Drexel, Morgan reorganized the big Philadelphia and Reading Railroad. This involved issuing new bonds with lower interest rates and assessing shareholders to lighten the burden on the line. The revived railroad was then taken over by a Morgan antagonist named A. Archibald McLeod, who later declared, “I would rather run a peanut-stand than be dictated to by J. P. Morgan.”[26](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2204643) He freely defied Morgan and invaded the territory of his other railroads. The experience would convince Pierpont not to release his grip on reorganized companies.
1880 年代，铁路行业的血雨腥风愈演愈烈。多家铁路公司命悬一线，险些破产。1886 年，德雷克塞尔-摩根公司重组了庞大的费城与雷丁铁路公司，通过发行低息新债券并向股东摊派费用，以减轻线路的财务负担。然而，这家起死回生的铁路公司随后落入摩根的死对头 A.阿奇博尔德·麦克劳德手中，他后来放出狠话：“我宁愿去摆个花生摊，也不愿受 J.P.摩根的指手画脚。” [26](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2204643) 他公然与摩根对着干，还蚕食他旗下其他铁路公司的地盘。这段经历让皮尔庞特彻底明白：重组后的公司绝不能撒手不管。

The basic weakness with America’s railroad system was overbuilding, which forced the roads into endless rounds of rate cuts and wage cuts to service debt. At the same time, the massive power of their largest consumers—notably Rockefeller in oil and Carnegie in steel—forced them to grant preferential rebates to big shippers, enraging small western farmers and businessmen and stimulating calls for government regulation. For Pierpont, the leading symbol of railway monopoly, pure competition was never an option. Years later, he said, “The American public seems to be unwilling to admit . . . that it has a choice between regulated legal agreements and unregulated extralegal agreements. We should have cast away more than 50 years ago the impossible doctrine of protection of the public by railway competition.”[27](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2204820) As we shall see repeatedly, the House of Morgan always favored government planning over private competition, but private planning over either.
美国铁路系统的根本弱点在于过度建设，这迫使各铁路公司为偿付债务而陷入无休止的降薪和降价的恶性循环。与此同时，其最大客户——尤其是石油业洛克菲勒和钢铁业卡内基——的庞大势力，迫使他们向大货主提供优惠回扣，这激怒了西部的小农场主和商人，引发了要求政府监管的呼声。对铁路垄断的象征性人物皮尔庞特而言，纯粹竞争从来不是选项。多年后他说道："美国公众似乎不愿承认……他们必须在受监管的合法协议与不受监管的非法协议之间做出选择。我们在 50 多年前就该抛弃那种通过铁路竞争来保护公众的不可信教条。" [27](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2204820) 正如我们将反复看到的那样，摩根财团始终偏爱政府规划胜过私人竞争，但更偏爱私人规划胜过这两者。

In 1887, Congress passed the Interstate Commerce Act, the first regulatory commission, which enshrined competition as its guiding principle and eliminated the controversial rebates. Supporters of the act formed a diverse constituency, ranging from small shippers to the railroads themselves; the latter accepted the inevitability of regulation and hoped that in the proper form it might provide some sorely needed stability. But within six months of the creation of the Interstate Commerce Commission, the rebates reappeared. Hence, in 1888 the railroad chieftains decided to graft their own form of self-regulation on the ICC framework under the aegis of Pierpont Morgan.
1887 年，美国国会通过了《州际商业法》，设立了首个监管委员会，将竞争奉为指导原则，并取消了备受争议的回扣制度。该法案的支持者来自不同群体，从小型托运人到铁路公司本身皆在其中；后者承认监管势在必行，并希望合理的监管形式能为行业带来亟需的稳定性。然而，州际商业委员会成立不到六个月，回扣现象便卷土重来。因此，1888 年，铁路巨头们决定在皮尔庞特·摩根的支持下，将一套自我监管机制嫁接至州际商业委员会的框架之上。

That December, newspaper readers were regaled with accounts of mysterious doings at Morgan’s Murray Hill home. As reporters staked out the house, they saw a procession of western railroad presidents and bankers disappear inside. Those arriving included Charles Francis Adams of the Union Pacific and a ghastly sick Jay Gould representing the Missouri Pacific. The Morgan house was under siege: reporters kept ringing the doorbell and fixed opera glasses on the windows. Inside, at the head of his library table, Pierpont opened the discussion with these words: “The purpose of this meeting is to cause the members of this association to no longer take the law into their own hands when they suspect they have been wronged, as has been too much the practice heretofore. . . . This is not elsewhere customary in civilized communities, and no good reason exists why such a practice should continue among railroads.”[28](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2204976) Clearly, Pierpont’s European experience formed his frame of reference.
那年十二月，报章读者津津乐道于摩根位于默里山的宅邸中发生的种种神秘事件。记者们在屋外蹲守时，目睹一列西部铁路公司总裁和银行家鱼贯而入。来访者中包括联合太平洋铁路公司的查尔斯·弗朗西斯·亚当斯，以及代表密苏里太平洋铁路公司、病容惨淡的杰伊·古尔德。摩根宅邸被重重围困：记者们不断按响门铃，还把观剧望远镜对准窗户。屋内，皮尔庞特坐在图书馆长桌的主位，以这番话开启了讨论：“本次会议的目的，是让本协会成员不再像以往那样，一自觉受冤便私自执法——这种行径此前已泛滥成灾……文明社会的其他领域并无此等惯例，铁路行业也绝无理由继续如此。” [28](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2204976) 显然，皮尔庞特在欧洲的经历塑造了他的参照标准。

Backed by representatives of Barings and Brown Brothers, Pierpont offered the railroad presidents a deal: if they refrained from rate-cutting and cutthroat competition, the financiers would stop underwriting competing railways. It was a clever move, for while Wall Street accused the railroads of irresponsible behavior, the railroads blamed Wall Street for floating too many securities and creating the overexpansion that led to price wars. Morgan himself was accused of sponsoring overcapitalized lines that couldn’t weather recessions because of their heavy debt load. The December 1888 meetings produced a gentleman’s agreement to maintain rates for sixty days; then the group would reassemble at Morgan’s house.
巴林银行和布朗兄弟的代表们背后支持的皮尔庞特向铁路大亨们提出了一项交易：如果他们停止降价和恶性竞争，金融家们将不再为同行铁路公司承销证券。这招颇为高明——华尔街指责铁路公司行为不负责任，而铁路公司则反呛华尔街发行了过多证券，导致过度扩张，最终引发价格战。摩根本人也被指控资助那些资本过度膨胀的铁路线，这些线路因债务高企而无法抵御经济衰退。1888 年 12 月的会议达成了一项绅士协议：维持费率六十天，之后这群人将再次在摩根家中会面。

A similar gathering took place at Pierpont’s “black library” in January 1889. This one yielded plans for a huge centralized group to regulate the entire rail system—the Interstate Commerce Railway Association. This behemoth would set rates, arbitrate disputes, and mete out fines to offending railroads. Pierpont was to head the cartel. The New York *Sun* called the new group “nothing short of a revolution in railroad methods.”[29](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2205163) But the new group soon fell apart under the pressure of western rate wars.
1889 年 1 月，皮尔庞特（Pierpont）的“黑色图书馆”也上演了一场类似的聚会。这次聚会催生了一个旨在监管整个铁路系统的庞大中央集团——州际商业铁路协会（Interstate Commerce Railway Association）。这个庞然大物将制定运价、仲裁纠纷，并对违规的铁路公司处以罚款。皮尔庞特将执掌这个垄断联盟。《纽约太阳报》（The New York Sun）称这个新组织“无异于铁路运营方式的一场革命”。 [29](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2205163) 但不久后，在西部运价战的冲击下，这个新组织便土崩瓦解了。

Pierpont’s last stab at establishing railroad stability took place at a meeting on December 15, 1890. Besides the earlier luminaries, this gathering drew Stuyvesant Fish of the Illinois Central, fames J. Hill of the Great Northern, and T. F. Oakes of the Northern Pacific. Pierpont presented a plan for a Western Traffic Association, which would include one director from each railroad and would set uniform rates; any railroad that cheated would be discharged. He was mightily pleased with his plan. In a rare burst of public candor, he exulted to a reporter, “Think of it—all the competing traffic of the roads west of Chicago and Saint Louis placed in the control of about 30 men!”[30](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2205337) The statement is splendidly innocent, yet perilously blind. Pierpont believed so implicitly in his own fairness and good judgment that he saw no harm in a large section of America’s economy coming under his personal dominion. The *New York Herald* blared, “RAILROAD KINGS FORM A GIGANTIC TRUST.”[31](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2205514) Before too long, this plan, too, would crumble.
1890 年 12 月 15 日，皮尔庞特在一场会议上为铁路行业稳定性做了最后一次尝试。与会的除早先的显要人物外，还有伊利诺伊中央铁路的斯泰弗森特·菲什、大北方铁路的詹姆斯·J·希尔，以及北太平洋铁路的 T·F·奥克斯。皮尔庞特提出组建西部交通协会的方案：每家铁路公司各派一名董事，统一制定运价，任何作弊的铁路公司将被除名。他对自己的计划得意至极，难得在公开场合坦言：“想想看——芝加哥和圣路易斯以西所有铁路的竞争性运输，都将控制在大约 30 人手中！”这句话天真得令人叫绝，却也危险地盲目。皮尔庞特深信自己的公正与判断力，竟觉得美国经济的一大片区域落入他个人掌控并无不妥。《纽约先驱报》则大声疾呼：“铁路大王们缔造了巨型托拉斯。”” [31](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2205514) 没过多久，这个计划也宣告破产了。

In the last analysis, the gentleman’s agreements suffered the historic fate of cartels. They couldn’t control small outside competitors, who cut rates, outflanked larger rivals, and won new business. With surreptitious cheating and lack of discipline, deals soon collapsed. Even the now-immense authority of Pierpont Morgan couldn’t solve the structural problems caused by too many railroads chasing too few passengers and owing too much money. As scores of railroads went bankrupt during the 1893 panic, Pierpont would reorganize many of them and use controversial new techniques to bring about order.
归根结底，这些君子协定还是逃不过卡特尔的历史宿命。他们管不住外面那些小打小闹的竞争对手——这帮人降低收费、迂回包抄大公司、抢走新业务。加上私下里偷奸耍滑、毫无纪律可言，协议很快就不攻自破。就连当时权势滔天的皮尔庞特·摩根，也解决不了这个结构性难题：铁路太多，乘客太少，债台高筑。1893 年恐慌期间，大批铁路公司倒闭，皮尔庞特一手重组了其中不少企业，并用一些充满争议的新手段来重振秩序。

This phase of Pierpont’s life shows that his real vice was not money but power. This was not power of a pathological sort, not power to bully men and bask in glory—though there was some of that—but power to take what he saw as a topsy-turvy financial world and set it right. Among robber barons, he was unique in suffering an excess of morality. He believed that he could master the problems of his era at a time when others were confused by the sheer dynamism and speed of economic change.
皮尔庞特这一阶段的人生表明，他真正的嗜好不是金钱，而是权力。这种权力并非病态的霸权，也不是欺压他人、享受荣耀的欲望——尽管其中不乏此类成分——而是一种将他认为混乱不堪的金融世界拨乱反正的力量。在那些强盗大亨中，他是唯一一个因道德过剩而显得与众不同的人。当其他人被经济变革的迅猛活力与速度搞得晕头转向时，他却相信自己能驾驭这个时代的难题。

As this new power accrued to the House of Morgan, making it the premier American bank, excruciating responsibility fell on Pierpont’s shoulders. Yet his office staff was slim, with only eighty employees. Pierpont didn’t even have a permanent secretary. Junius continued to warn his son against exhausting immersion in business. At the same time, his secretive merchant-banker’s sensibility was shocked when Pierpont appointed a clerk to open incoming mail. In the late 1880s, in a final volley of advice, Junius wrote that “no body, however strong & well he may be, can stand such strain upon his physical and mental powers as you have had for the last 2 years without paying sooner or later the penalty unless he gives them a *real rest* & gives it to them *in* season.”[32](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2205698) Yet Junius never saw how much his own unbending style and unrealistically high standards had contributed to Pierpont’s slavish dedication to work.
随着摩根家族权势的日益膨胀，它一跃成为美国首屈一指的银行，而皮尔庞特肩上却压下了令人窒息的重担。然而他的办公室人员精简，仅有八十名员工，甚至连个固定的秘书都没有。朱尼乌斯不断告诫儿子，不要全身心扑在生意上劳神伤身。与此同时，当皮尔庞特指派一名职员负责拆阅来信时，朱尼乌斯那种隐秘的商人银行家敏感神经顿时备受冲击。19 世纪 80 年代末，在最后一轮忠告中，朱尼乌斯写道："无论一个人多么强壮健康，若像你过去两年那样持续透支身心，迟早都要付出代价——除非他适时给自己真正的休息。" [32](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2205698) 然而朱尼乌斯从未意识到，正是他自己那种固执己见的作风和不切实际的高标准，才造就了皮尔庞特对工作的奴隶般奉献。

BY the 1880s, as his health was fading, Junius Morgan slowly eased out of business. The Iron Duke of the Morgan saga had become the most influential American banker in London, a peer of Barings and Rothschilds, his firm participating in an international smorgasbord of loans—for the Egyptian national bank, Russian railways, Brazilian provincial governments, and Argentinean public works. Whatever his health problems, he gave an impression of rock-solid durability; the London *Times* declared him “a hale and vigorous man, for his years.”[33](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2205853)
到 19 世纪 80 年代，随着健康状况每况愈下，朱尼厄斯·摩根（Junius Morgan）逐渐退出了商界。这位摩根家族中的"铁血公爵"已成为伦敦最具影响力的美国银行家，与巴林银行（Barings）和罗斯柴尔德家族（Rothschilds）平起平坐，他的公司参与了一盘国际贷款的"大杂烩"——从埃及国家银行、俄国铁路、巴西各省级政府，到阿根廷公共工程，无所不包。纵使身体抱恙，他仍给人以坚如磐石的印象；《伦敦泰晤士报》称他为"虽年事已高，却精神矍铄、体格硬朗之人"。

In 1884, Junius’s wife, Juliet, died at the age of sixty-eight. Surrounded by her favorite collection of china dogs, she had been, as the Morgan family tactfully phrased it, “confused” in her later years and confined to an upstairs room much of the time. Thus, she had been unable to share in her husband’s life. After her death, Junius’s solitude was relieved by twice-weekly letters from Pierpont and visits from his grandchildren. J. P. Morgan, Jr., whom the family called Jack, worshiped his grandfather and particularly liked the English formality at 13 Princes Gate, including the way the servants treated him as “heir apparent.”[34](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2206000) Junius was as attached as ever to Pierpont. After a visit from him in the south of France, he wrote, “Pierpont & family left today—House very lonely—miss them dreadfully.”[35](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2206190)
1884 年，朱尼厄斯的妻子朱丽叶去世，享年六十八岁。晚年时，她整日与收藏的瓷器狗为伴，用摩根家族委婉的说法，她一直"神志不清"，大部分时间都被关在楼上的房间里。因此，她无法与丈夫共度晚年。朱丽叶去世后，朱尼厄斯靠皮尔庞特每周两次的来信和孙辈们的探访来缓解孤独。家族中昵称为杰克的 J.P.摩根二世十分崇拜祖父，尤其喜欢王子门 13 号那种英式的正式氛围，包括仆人们视他为"法定继承人"的待客之道。 [34](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2206000) 朱尼厄斯对皮尔庞特的依恋一如既往。皮尔庞特从法国南部来访离开后，他写道："皮尔庞特和家人今天走了——房子变得非常冷清——想他们想得不行。" [35](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2206190)

These visits were Junius’s main pleasure at the end. A photograph of him taken in 1890 shows the firm mouth and steady gaze of earlier years. His hair was snow-white, his eyebrows white and tufty, and the top of his head was bald. He spent winters at the Villa Henriette in Monte Carlo, which had a beautiful view of the Mediterranean. Leading an orderly, bourgeois life, he dined with friends and took afternoon carriage drives. During one excursion on the afternoon of April 3, 1890, the horses were startled by an onrushing train. Junius jumped up to see whether his coachman could master the team. At that instant, the carriage ran against a heap of stones and flung him violently against a wall, breaking his wrist and causing a brain concussion. For five days, he lay unconscious. Then the flow of maxims ceased forever. Perhaps it was appropriate that Junius’s death was dealt by one stunning blow in his seventy-seventh year rather than by a dribbling away of strength,-in its obituary notice, the London *Times* remarked that he had hardly been ill in his life.[36](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2206345) Certainly there was mysterious symbolism in the fact that a train’s sudden roar, upsetting a pastoral landscape, had killed one of London’s foremost railroad bankers.
这些会面成为了朱尼厄斯晚年最大的慰藉。一张 1890 年拍摄的照片中，他依然保持着早年那坚毅的嘴唇和沉稳的目光。他的头发已如雪般洁白，眉毛也是白色而浓密，头顶早已光秃。冬天他住在蒙特卡洛的亨丽埃特别墅，那里可以俯瞰美丽的地中海景色。他过着秩序井然的资产阶级生活，与朋友共进晚餐，午后乘坐马车兜风。1890 年 4 月 3 日下午的一次出游中，马匹被一辆疾驰而来的火车惊扰。朱尼厄斯猛地起身，想看看车夫是否能控制住马群。就在那一刻，马车撞上了一堆石头，将他猛烈地甩向墙壁，导致手腕骨折和脑震荡。他昏迷了五天。随后，那些格言警句的流淌永远停止了。 或许正合宜的是，朱尼厄斯在七十七岁那年被一记重击夺去生命，而非逐渐衰弱而逝。《伦敦泰晤士报》在其讣告中写道，他一生几乎从未生过病。 [36](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2206345) 火车突如其来的轰鸣，扰乱了田园风光，竟夺去了伦敦一位顶级铁路银行家的性命，这其中无疑蕴含着某种神秘的象征意义。

Junius was buried in the Cedar Hill Cemetery in Hartford. As he had for Peabody, Pierpont devised a funeral suitable for an illustrious warrior-hero. Hartford shopkeepers along the funeral route closed their businesses for the occasion, while flags flew at half-mast over the state capitol. Pierpont’s inscription to Junius for the Morgan Memorial Building at the Wadsworth Atheneum said much about their common identification with London’s merchant-banking tradition: “In loving memory of Junius Spencer Morgan, a native of Massachusetts, a merchant of Hartford . . . afterwards a merchant of London.”[37](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2206492)
朱尼厄斯被安葬在哈特福德的雪松山公墓。正如他为皮博迪所做的那样，皮尔庞特为这位杰出的勇士英雄策划了一场与之相称的葬礼。葬礼路线沿途的哈特福德商铺为此关门歇业，州议会大厦降半旗致哀。皮尔庞特为沃兹沃斯学会的摩根纪念楼题写的朱尼厄斯铭文，充分体现了他们对伦敦商人银行传统的共同认同：“谨以此纪念朱尼厄斯·斯宾塞·摩根，生于马萨诸塞州，曾是哈特福德的一名商人……后成为伦敦的一名商人。” [37](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2206492)

Did Pierpont resent his father’s domination? Or was his admiration as unmixed as he claimed? Whatever anger or ambivalence he felt was buried beneath gigantic monuments. He honored Junius like Hamlet’ mourning the dead king. For twelve years, he gathered up land around Hartford’s Wadsworth Atheneum in order to create the Morgan Memorial, a \$1.4-million pink marble building in English Renaissance style that doubled the museum’s size. Years later—glancing impatiently at his pocket watch all the while—he surveyed blueprints and rapidly picked out three new buildings for the Harvard Medical School, again to certify a son’s love. And upon the red damask wall of the West Room of his own library, Junius’s portrait would hold pride of place, ringed by Umbrian Madonnas and infant Saviors—the powerful patriarch surrounded by loving children and ethereal females. After a small fire at his Madison Avenue townhouse, Pierpont was asked which treasure he would have rescued first. “My father’s portrait,” he said without hesitation.
皮尔庞特是否憎恨父亲的掌控？还是像他自称的那样，对父亲毫无保留地崇拜？无论他内心掩藏着多少愤怒与矛盾，这一切都被巨大的纪念碑深埋于地下。他纪念朱尼厄斯的方式，如同哈姆雷特哀悼逝去的君王。整整十二年，他不断收购哈特福德沃兹沃思学会周边的土地，最终建成了耗资 140 万美元的摩根纪念堂——一座粉红色大理石的英国文艺复兴风格建筑，让博物馆的规模扩大了一倍。多年后，他一边不耐烦地瞥着怀表，一边审视蓝图，迅速为哈佛医学院选定了三座新楼，这同样是为了印证一个儿子对父亲的爱。而在自己图书馆西厅的红色锦缎墙上，朱尼厄斯的肖像占据着最显赫的位置，四周环绕着翁布里亚圣母与圣婴画像——这位威严的大家长，被慈爱的子女和缥缈的女性形象所簇拥。麦迪逊大道的联排别墅曾发生一场小火，事后有人问皮尔庞特，如果真要抢救，他会先带出哪件珍宝。 “我父亲的画像，”他毫不犹豫地说道。

An American magazine had recently listed Pierpont and Junius as among America’s richest men. Now Pierpont inherited an estate of \$12.4 million, and his personal fortune doubled overnight. Ten million dollars would stay in the bank. He was bequeathed control of a banking empire and assumed his father’s position in the City. Like his father, he stood astride that flow of capital from Britain to America and would profit as it reversed direction in the new century.
近日，一家美国杂志将皮尔庞特和朱尼厄斯列为美国最富有的人之一。如今，皮尔庞特继承了 1240 万美元的遗产，个人财富一夜之间翻了一番。其中一千万美元将存入银行。他还获得了一个银行帝国的控制权，并接替了父亲在伦敦金融城的地位。同他父亲一样，他横跨英美之间的资本流动，并将在新世纪资本流向逆转时从中获利。

After Junius’s death, some shackle was lifted from Pierpont’s spirit. A new grandiosity flowered and he self-consciously became J. Pierpont Morgan, mogul, pirate, patron of the arts. Before Junius’s death, Pierpont’s collections were modest; in 1888, he had bought his first literary manuscript, a Thackeray. Now he embarked on a buying spree that would eventually produce the world’s largest art collection in private hands. To trumpet the new J. P. Morgan, he also enlisted his friend J. Frederic Tams to design *Corsair II.* Tams was given blank Drexel, Morgan checks and told to forget about expense; the only restriction was that the boat be able to turn around in the Hudson River near Cragston. A dark, sleek ship with a glamorous black hull and yellow smokestack, this new *Corsair* measured over two hundred and forty-one feet in length and aggressively laid claim to the title of the largest pleasure vessel afloat. In time, the mere appearance of the *Corsair II* in foreign harbors would alarm the populace, as if warning of an impending invasion of American capital.
朱尼厄斯去世后，皮尔庞特精神上的桎梏仿佛被解开了。一股前所未有的豪情喷薄而出，他开始自觉地以 J·皮尔庞特·摩根自居——既是巨头、海盗，也是艺术赞助人。在父亲去世前，皮尔庞特的收藏还相当有限；1888 年，他购入了第一份文学手稿——萨克雷的作品。如今他开启了疯狂采购模式，最终打造出世界上规模最大的私人艺术收藏。为了彰显焕然一新的 J·P·摩根，他还委托友 J·弗雷德里克·塔姆斯设计“科尔萨二号”。塔姆斯拿到空白支票（德雷塞尔与摩根联合账户），被告知不必考虑成本；唯一限制是这艘船必须在克拉格斯顿附近的哈德逊河上完成掉头。这艘暗色流线型游艇拥有魅惑的黑色船体和黄色烟囱，全长超过二百四十一英尺，霸气地宣告自己是当时最大的私人游艇。 久而久之，只要“海盗二号”出现在外国港口，就会引起民众恐慌，仿佛是在警示美国资本的入侵即将来临。

THE men in the Morgan family might have been far happier had not each of three consecutive generations produced only one son to survive to adulthood. In merchant-banking families, the whole weight of the dynasty was at once placed on the male infants. Unlike publicly traded companies, which have a corporate life of their own, private merchant-bank partnerships often relied upon the name, capital, and reputation of a single family. If the male heir(s) refused to go into the family business, it might have to be wound up. Thus, Morgan expectations were lodged first by Junius in Pierpont, then by Pierpont in Jack. In both cases, business pressures would tremendously intensify the typical father-son tensions.
摩根家族的男人们或许本可以更加幸福，若非连续三代都只育有一名男婴且存活至成年。在商业银行家族中，整个王朝的重担瞬间压在了男性继承人身上。与拥有独立企业生命的上市公司不同，私人商业银行合伙制企业往往依赖单一家族的名望、资本和声誉。如果男性继承人拒绝继承家业，企业可能就要被迫解散。因此，摩根家族的期望先是朱尼厄斯寄托于皮尔庞特，而后皮尔庞特又寄托于杰克。在这两代人中，商业压力极大地加剧了本已紧张的父子关系。

From the outset, Pierpont’s relationship with Jack differed from his own with Junius. If Pierpont suffered from Junius’s sometimes smothering attention, Jack suffered the curse of neglect. He craved the love of a father who seemed too remote and too self-absorbed to attend to his boyish needs. Between Jack and his father there would always be some distance, some nameless discomfort, that was very different from the intense, manly mutual fascination between Junius and Pierpont. Both Pierpont and Jack were shy and clumsy and steeped in New England formality. It was difficult for the delicate, insecure Jack to cope with the great flashing, roaring engine of a famous father.
从一开始，皮尔庞特与杰克的关系就和他与朱尼厄斯的关系截然不同。如果说朱尼厄斯那有时令人窒息的关注让皮尔庞特备受折磨，那么杰克则承受着被忽视的诅咒。他渴望得到父爱，可父亲似乎太过疏远、太过自我，根本无暇顾及他那些少年心性的需求。在杰克和父亲之间，永远存在着某种距离，某种难以言说的隔阂，这与朱尼厄斯和皮尔庞特之间那种激烈而阳刚的相互吸引截然不同。皮尔庞特和杰克都腼腆笨拙，深受新英格兰式的拘谨影响。对于敏感而不安的杰克来说，要面对这位如轰鸣闪光引擎般大名鼎鼎的父亲，实在是一件难事。

Unlike Pierpont, who had been a wild, headstrong boy requiring a firm hand, Jack needed a father to buck up his faltering courage—which Pierpont didn’t do. Jack was gentle and sedentary, lacking fire. He attended Saint Paul’s School in Concord, New Hampshire, where rich adolescent boys were exposed to Spartan Yankee routines. They had to write weekly letters home but couldn’t receive presents and had to seek pocket money from the rector. Where Pierpont wrote boyhood essays in praise of Napoléon, Jack seemed more protective of the weak. Explaining why one teacher was his favorite, he confessed: “I suppose that it is partly because I feel sorrier for him than any of the others—the boys do plague him so.”[38](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2206652) In 1880, at thirteen, he cried upon reading *Dom-bey and Son*, Dickens’s novel about a stern magnate father and his sensitive son. Like his own father, Jack suffered migraine headaches that lasted for days. Big, awkward, and docile, Jack liked well-bred boys, not ruffians, and already sounded middle-aged at twelve, telling Fanny he refrained from marbles because “it doesn’t pay for the wear and tear and chapping of the knuckles.”[39](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2206843)
与皮尔庞特不同——那个需要严加管束的桀骜少年——杰克需要的是父亲能为他摇摇欲坠的勇气打气，而皮尔庞特并未做到。杰克性情温和、喜静不喜动，缺乏锋芒。他就读于新罕布什尔州康科德的圣保罗学校，那里富家少年要接受斯巴达式的严苛训练。他们必须每周给家里写信，不能收礼物，零花钱也得向校长申请。皮尔庞特少年时就写文章歌颂拿破仑，而杰克似乎更懂得保护弱者。在解释为何偏爱某位老师时，他坦言："我想部分原因是我比其他人更同情他——那些男孩实在太折磨他了。"1880 年，十三岁的他读狄更斯描写严厉大亨父亲与敏感儿子的小说《董贝父子》时潸然泪下。和父亲一样，杰克也遭受持续数日的偏头痛折磨。 杰克身材高大，笨拙温顺，喜欢有教养的男孩，而不是粗野的孩子。他十二岁就说话像个中年人，告诉芬妮他不玩弹珠，因为“手指关节磨损皲裂，实在不划算。”” [39](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2206843) ”

Jack lacked the nerve to contest his terrifying, distant father. Where Pierpont had the fortitude to confront Junius, Jack silently hoped for approval and leaned on his mother for emotional support. He found his father a man of violent and mercurial moods. His anxiety grew especially acute about money, a subject invested with many family taboos. Like the young Pierpont, Jack kept strict accounts of his expenses. We find him recording ten cents for a library fine at school and charging expenses against his “Christmas money” or “grandpa money.”[40](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2207018) Whenever the subjects of Pierpont and money coincided, Jack trembled: “You see I don’t mean to do anything about money that Papa wouldn’t like,” he told his mother. “Papa hates so to have me come to him about money matters that I did not mean in any way to hint that he ought to pay the bill.”[41](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2207196) Such sentiments abound in his boyhood letters.
杰克缺乏胆量去挑战他那令人生畏且疏远的父亲。皮尔庞特有勇气直面朱尼厄斯，而杰克却默默地渴望得到认可，并依赖母亲寻求情感支持。他发现父亲是个性情暴烈、喜怒无常的人。他对金钱问题尤为焦虑，而这一话题在家庭中又充满诸多禁忌。和年轻的皮尔庞特一样，杰克严格记录自己的开销。我们能看到他记下在学校图书馆罚款十美分，并从“圣诞钱”或“爷爷钱”中列支费用。 [40](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2207018) 每当皮尔庞特和金钱这两个话题重合时，杰克就会颤抖：“你看，我不想做任何爸爸不喜欢的事，”他告诉母亲，“爸爸非常讨厌我跟他谈钱的事，我根本没打算暗示他应该付账。” [41](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2207196) 这样的情绪在他少年时期的书信中比比皆是。

Jack’s letters to his mother form the most complete record of Morgan family life; unfortunately, no account from Fanny’s side remains. It is clear, however, that Jack was passionately attached to his mother. Sensitive to each other’s melancholy, they shared the great enigma of J. Pierpont Morgan and consoled each other for forty years. Later on, we shall see Jack Morgan as a bitter old man, yet here he was as an ardent boy, bursting with affection, telling his mother: “Dear, I love you as you know and just now I am full of comfort thinking I am going to see you in less than a week.”[42](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2207389) Even as a teenager, he felt protective toward Fanny and sometimes sounded more like parent than child. As Fanny became depressed and bedridden—there are many references to her invalidism in Jack’s letters—he tried to cheer her up. In 1889, he wrote, “As to your blues,--I can only say, what every one else does, do take care enough not to overtire yourself, and watch against them all you know how.”[43](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2207583) As a teenager, he was slightly puzzled when a friend’s mother described Fanny as “calm cold unenthusiastic.”[44](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2207776) Yet the episode suggests that Fanny may have been aloof in the outside world and showed her emotions only in private.
杰克写给母亲的信，为我们留下了摩根家族生活最完整的记录；遗憾的是，芬妮方面没有任何资料留存。但显而易见，杰克对母亲情感深厚。他们彼此体察对方的忧郁，共同面对 J·皮尔庞特·摩根这个巨大的谜团，四十年来互相慰藉。日后我们将看到杰克·摩根成为一位刻薄的老人，而此刻他仍是个热情的少年，满怀爱意地对母亲倾诉：“亲爱的，你知道我爱你，此刻想到不到一周就能见到你，我心中满是慰藉。” [42](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2207389) 即便在十几岁时，他就对芬妮充满保护欲，有时说话的语气更像家长而非孩子。当芬妮陷入抑郁、卧病在床——杰克信中多次提及她的体弱多病——他总想方设法逗她开心。1889 年他写道：“至于你的忧郁——我只能像所有人那样对你说，千万注意别让自己太过劳累，用你知道的一切方法来提防它们。”“作为一个青少年，当他朋友的母亲形容范妮‘冷静、冷淡、不热情’时，他感到有些困惑。然而，这件事表明范妮可能在外人面前显得疏离，只有在私下才会表露情感。”

While Pierpont had a smattering of university education at Göttingen, Jack was the first Morgan to obtain a college degree, graduating from Harvard in 1889. He had a broad, smooth face, with dark hair flattened on top, and a mustache. His Harvard years, which coincided with his father’s gentleman’s agreements, were free of rebellion. While Pierpont knocked heads with railroad satraps in New York, Jack loafed, smoked pipes, and took a gentleman’s C, spending his senior year studying the properties of seaweed. It was symptomatic of Jack’s humility and his insecurity that when he made an exciting discovery in his laboratory, he chalked it off it to luck.
虽然皮尔庞特在哥廷根大学接受过零星的大学教育，但杰克是摩根家族中第一个获得大学学位的人，他于 1889 年毕业于哈佛大学。他脸庞宽阔光滑，深色头发平整地梳在头顶，留着胡子。他在哈佛的岁月恰好与他父亲的君子协定时期重合，期间没有表现出任何叛逆。当皮尔庞特在纽约与铁路巨头们针锋相对时，杰克则悠闲度日，抽着烟斗，拿了个绅士般的 C 等成绩，大四那年都在研究海藻的特性。杰克的谦逊和不安全感在他于实验室做出激动人心的发现后归结为运气时体现得淋漓尽致。

Like his mother, Jack enjoyed literature, but seemed unsettled by dark worldviews. Proper and squeamish, he was disturbed by *Faust’s* tragic ending and found *La dame aux camélias* depressing. There would be no tubercular Mimi or tear-stained adventures in Jack’s young life. Sailing to Europe in 1887, he wrote, “There is only one girl on board who could be called a belle and I have kept very clear of her because she struck me as being very *common.*”[45](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2207970) He flirted with no dangerous doctrines and was already impatient with meddlesome people who stirred up trouble. “I don’t know why so many people . . . seem to look upon business as if it were the general sewer in which all ambition and intelligence disappear. I must confess I don’t see any harm myself in making a little money, provided that it can be done honestly and reasonably.”[46](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2208133) He was also quite religious. Where other young men hotly debated the justice of the social order, Jack worried about whether gambling should be openly denounced from the pulpit.
和他母亲一样，杰克也热爱文学，但似乎对阴暗的世界观感到不安。他循规蹈矩又有些矫情，觉得《Faust》的悲剧结局让人不舒服，《La dame aux camélias》也太过压抑。在杰克年轻的岁月里，不会有患肺结核的 Mimi，也不会有泪迹斑斑的冒险。1887 年乘船去欧洲时，他写道：“船上唯一算得上美女的女孩，我刻意跟她保持距离，因为我觉得她太俗气了。” [45](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2207970) 他不沾染任何危险的信条，对那些爱管闲事、惹是生非的人早已不耐烦。“真不明白为什么那么多人……把做生意看作是一切野心和才智消失的排污沟。我得承认，只要正正当当、合情合理地赚点钱，我并不觉得有什么不好。” [46](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2208133) 而且他那时还挺有信仰的。 其他年轻人热衷于争论社会秩序的公正性时，杰克却在担心赌博是否该在布道坛上被公开谴责。

Jack has left a melancholy record of the emotional chasm that separated him from his father. He told one satiric story that also said much about Pierpont’s self-absorption. He had invited a Harvard classmate to visit him at Cragston, and the young man rode up on the *Corsair* with Pierpont. After introductions, Pierpont promptly buried himself in the newspaper. When they landed, he said to Jack about the classmate, “That is one of the nicest young fellows I’ve met.”[47](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2208267)
杰克留下了一段感伤的记录，讲述了他与父亲之间难以逾越的情感鸿沟。他讲了一个讽刺的故事，也充分体现了皮尔庞特的自恋。他邀请了一位哈佛同学来克雷格斯顿做客，那年轻人是乘皮尔庞特的“海盗”号游艇来的。介绍过后，皮尔庞特立刻埋头看起报纸来。上岸后，他对杰克说起那位同学：“这是我见过的最讨人喜欢的年轻人之一。” [47](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2208267)

Pierpont apparently found Jack soft and rather passive, lacking the sort of gumption he had as a young man. In 1884 and 1885, he arranged for his son to take a hunting trip in the Rockies with William Rainsford, the rector of Saint George’s, who was a great sportsman. Jack shot a bighorn sheep and slept in a snowbound cabin—manly pursuits Pierpont hoped would toughen the young man up. Meanwhile, Jack’s intimate life remained confined to his mother.
皮尔庞特显然觉得杰克性格软糯、过于被动，缺少自己年轻时的那股闯劲。1884 到 1885 年间，他安排儿子跟着圣乔治教堂的教区长威廉·雷恩斯福德去落基山脉打猎——这位教区长可是个运动健将。杰克射杀了一只大角羊，还在被大雪封住的小木屋里过夜——这些充满阳刚气的经历，皮尔庞特希望能让儿子变得硬朗些。可与此同时，杰克的情感世界依然困在母亲身边。

In 1889, Jack graduated from Harvard and met Jane Norton Grew, daughter of Boston banker and mill owner Henry Sturgis Grew. Descended from several prominent families, including the Sturgises and the Wigglesworths, Jessie, as Jane was called, had a proper Bostonian pedigree. Yet before approving the match, the Morgans and the Grews circled around each other and sniffed for a while. Jack passed along Jessie’s genealogy to the snobbish Pierpont and kept requesting a chance to discuss their possible marriage. Finally Pierpont consented to talk with his son during his next trip to Boston. In a letter both angry and wistful, Jack told Fanny what happened:
1889 年，杰克从哈佛毕业，结识了简·诺顿·格鲁——波士顿银行家兼工厂主亨利·斯特吉斯·格鲁的女儿。简出身显赫家族，包括斯特吉斯家族和威格尔斯沃思家族，身世纯正的波士顿名门血统。不过，在摩根家族与格鲁家族正式缔结婚约前，双方还是互相试探了一番。杰克把简的家族谱系递给了势利的皮尔庞特，并一再请求能有机会商讨这桩可能的婚事。最终，皮尔庞特同意在下一次去波士顿时与儿子谈谈。在给范妮的信中，杰克既愤怒又惆怅地写道：

> On Saturday Papa telegraphed me he should be in Boston a few hours and hoped to see me. He was to arrive at 6:40 and go back at midnight, with a party of twelve for a *Corsair* dinner. I expected to be nearly an hour with him, instead of which his train was delayed and instead of seeing him I waited under a railroad bridge in the rain for an hour, and had the delightful opportunity of driving from the Station to the Club with him in the same carriage with Mr. Bowdoin \[Pierpont’s partner\] and Mr. Depew \[then president of the New York Central\]. As he had not sent me on a single one of your telegrams, and had not told me anything about Rainsford’s plans or even if he himself was certain to sail on Wednesday the visit was somewhat unsatisfactory. There certainly are some drawbacks to belonging to a busy man no matter how fine he may be as I believe you have sometimes found out.[48](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2208452)
> 周六父亲发电报给我，说他会在波士顿停留几个小时，希望见见我。他会在 6:40 抵达，午夜返回，同行的还有十二个人，要去参加一个海盗主题的晚宴。我以为能和他待将近一个小时，结果火车晚点了，我非但没见到他，反而在一座铁路桥下淋着雨等了一个小时。最后，我总算有机会和他同乘一辆马车从车站驶向俱乐部，车上还有鲍登先生\[皮尔庞特的合伙人\]和德普先生\[时任纽约中央铁路总裁\]。但他没有转发你发给我的任何一封电报，也没告诉我雷恩斯福德的计划，甚至自己是否确定周三出发都没提，这次见面多少有些令人失望。属于一个忙碌的男人确实有些不便，无论他多么优秀——我想你有时也深有体会。 [48](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2208452)

Most revealing is how the letter ends—with Jack portraying himself and Fanny as common victims of Pierpont. A month later, anxious and trembling, Jack blurted out the facts of the situation with Jessie. Pierpont responded that in the spring he and Fanny would consider the matter. Frightened of his father, Jack was always relieved and grateful when he received sympathetic attention. After a subsequent meeting, he told his mother, “It would be hard for me to exaggerate my thankfulness for the way in which Papa received my confidences, and the satisfaction I feel in having spoken to him. It has made me less blue than I have been for months.”[49](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2208643) On December 11, 1890, Jack and Jessie were wed in Boston’s Arlington Street Church, a marriage that made the front page of the *New York Times.*
最有意思的是信末的笔锋——杰克将自己和范妮描绘成皮尔庞特手下的同病相怜之人。一个月后，杰克惶惶不安地吐露了与杰西之间的实情。皮尔庞特回应说，等春天到了，他和范妮会考虑这件事。杰克向来惧怕父亲，所以每当得到父亲体恤的回应时，他总会如释重负、感激不尽。后来一次会面结束后，他对母亲说：“我实在难以形容，当父亲接纳我的坦白时，我心中是何等感激；把心里话告诉他之后，我感到无比欣慰。这让我几个月来从未如此释然。” [49](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2208643) 1890 年 12 月 11 日，杰克与杰西在波士顿的阿灵顿街教堂完婚，这场婚礼登上了《纽约时报》的头版。

The oral history that has come down through the Morgan family contends that Jack wanted to be a doctor and became a banker only when his father made it a matter of family honor.[50](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2208817) In 1892, at the age of twenty-five, Jack became a partner in the Morgan banks in New York, Philadelphia, and Paris. During a twenty-year business association, Jack would remain a close observer of his father, charting his manic-depressive moods and giving him more generous sympathy than he received in return, although the relationship would become somewhat more equal toward the end of Pierpont’s life.
根据摩根家族流传的口述历史，杰克原本想当医生，是父亲把这事提升到家族荣誉的高度，他才转而成了银行家。 [50](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2208817) 1892 年，25 岁的杰克成为摩根家族在纽约、费城和巴黎三家银行合伙人。在长达二十年的商业合作中，杰克始终密切关注着父亲的一举一动，记录他躁郁交替的情绪波动，给予的同情远比得到的回报更多——尽管在皮尔庞特生命末期，两人的关系总算变得稍显平等了些。

Jack entered the Morgan empire at a critical time. In June 1893, Tony Drexel died while visiting the Austro-Hungarian health resort of Karlsbad, leaving an estate said to be worth between \$25 and \$30 million. While giving Pierpont managerial control in New York, the Drexel family had retained control of Drexel and Company in Philadelphia and Drexel, Harjes in Paris. In October 1893, Anthony Drexel, Jr., decided to retire and devote himself to society pleasures, thus enabling Pierpont to strengthen his hold over the interlocking partnerships in New York, Philadelphia, Paris, and London. At a dinner meeting at the Metropolitan Club—the sole time in Morgan history that the New York and Philadelphia partners sat in one room—he announced a new plan for centralized control.
杰克在关键时刻进入了摩根帝国。1893 年 6 月，托尼·德雷克塞尔在奥匈帝国的疗养胜地卡尔斯巴德去世，留下了据称价值 2500 万至 3000 万美元的遗产。尽管德雷克塞尔家族将纽约的管理权交给了皮尔庞特，但他们仍保留了费城的德雷克塞尔公司以及巴黎的德雷克塞尔-哈耶斯公司的控制权。1893 年 10 月，小安东尼·德雷克塞尔决定退休，投身于社交享乐，这使得皮尔庞特得以加强对纽约、费城、巴黎和伦敦相互关联的合伙企业的掌控。在大都会俱乐部的一次晚宴上——这是摩根历史上唯一一次纽约和费城的合伙人同处一室——他宣布了一项集中管理的新计划。

In the 1895 reorganization, Drexel, Morgan was rechristened J. P. Morgan and Company, while the Paris office became Morgan, Harjes. The Philadelphia house remained Drexel and Company, but the Drexel family passed from the scene, and Pierpont tapped Edward T. Stotesbury, son of a Philadelphia sugar refiner, to head the Philadelphia office. J. S. Morgan and Company in London would soon undergo a major reorganization of personnel. Among the four Morgan partnerships, the only common denominator would be Pierpont’s position as all-powerful senior partner; his associates, in contrast, might be partners in some, but not all the firms. Pierpont would take 35 percent of the profits of the combined houses. Power had now passed from London to New York, which would remain the command post of the Morgan empire. Despite its multinational veneer, the Morgan empire would be American-based, with partners at 23 Wall wielding disproportionate power. Where Junius had dispatched Pierpont to New York as the lesser financial center, so Pierpont would dispatch Jack to London, soon to be eclipsed by New York. On the eve of an unprecedented industrial boom in America, which would see the creation of vast trusts, the House of Morgan had opportunely shifted its center of gravity westward across the Atlantic.
在 1895 年的重组中，Drexel, Morgan 更名为 J. P. Morgan and Company，而巴黎办事处则改为 Morgan, Harjes。费城的公司仍保留 Drexel and Company 的名称，但 Drexel 家族逐渐退出舞台，Pierpont 任命费城糖业大亨之子 Edward T. Stotesbury 负责费城办事处。伦敦的 J. S. Morgan and Company 很快将进行重大人事调整。在四家摩根合伙公司中，唯一的共同点是 Pierpont 作为大权在握的高级合伙人——他的合伙人可能同时参与其中几家，但并非全部。Pierpont 将抽取联合公司 35%的利润。权力中心已从伦敦转移到纽约，后者将继续作为摩根帝国的指挥中枢。尽管披着跨国公司的外衣，摩根帝国本质上扎根美国，而华尔街 23 号的合伙人则掌握着不成比例的巨大权力。 朱尼厄斯曾将皮尔庞特派往当时次要的金融中心纽约，而皮尔庞特又将杰克派往即将被纽约超越的伦敦。在美国即将迎来前所未有的工业繁荣、庞大的托拉斯即将兴起之际，摩根财团适时地将重心向西跨越大西洋转移。

PIERPONT Morgan’s thunderous presence at 23 Wall Street could be observed by visitors as soon as they entered his glass-enclosed, wood-paneled offices. (The concept was copied from Junius’s office.) Seated in a swivel chair before a rolltop desk on the Broad Street side, a coal fire behind him in winter, he would rise, stroll over, and question his partners as he needed to. Lincoln Steffens recalled how he sat in a back room with glass sides and the door open. This sense of access was illusory, however, for his imperious stare could reduce interlopers to jelly. He unnerved those who overstayed a visit by simply writing and not looking up. Steffens recalled that “his partners did not go near him unless he sent for them; and then they looked alarmed and darted in like office boys.”[51](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2209005) Even his partners called him Mr. Morgan, or the Senior. So there he sat, displayed like a carnival waxwork, the man Bernard Baruch termed “the greatest financial genius this country has ever known.”[52](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2209161) He invited intimacy but then rebuffed it; his aura was so fearsome that crowds parted before him on the pavement. Once, when an Episcopal bishop visited Cragston, Pierpont was able to flag down a West Shore train in the middle of the night so the prelate could make his way back to Manhattan.
皮尔庞特·摩根在华尔街 23 号那雷霆万钧的气场，访客一踏入他那玻璃围挡、镶木护墙的办公室就能感受到。（这设计理念是仿效朱尼厄斯的办公室。）他坐在百老汇街一侧的一张卷盖式书桌前，冬日里身后燃着煤火，会站起身踱步过来，根据需要询问合伙人。林肯·斯蒂芬斯回忆说，他坐在一间四面玻璃、房门敞开的里间。但这种“触手可及”的感觉不过是假象——他那咄咄逼人的目光足以让不速之客吓得瘫软。他只需埋头写字不抬头，就能让那些赖着不走的人坐立不安。斯蒂芬斯回忆道：“他的合伙人除非他传唤，否则绝不敢靠近他；即便被叫去，也个个神色慌张，像办公室勤杂工一样冲进去。” [51](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2209005) 连合伙人都称他为“摩根先生”，或“大当家”。他就那样端坐着，宛如嘉年华上的蜡像——此人正是伯纳德·巴鲁克口中“本国史上最伟大的金融天才”。 [52](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2209161) 他先是邀人亲近，转而又拒人千里；气场之强悍，令行人道上的人群见了他纷纷避让。 有一次，一位圣公会主教到访克莱格斯顿，皮尔庞特竟能在深更半夜拦下一列西岸铁路的火车，好让这位主教赶回曼哈顿。

There are many stories of Pierpont’s brusque impatience and his economy of self-expression. He had a short attention span and sometimes worked only from eleven o’clock to three or four in the afternoon, pausing for a sandwich, pie, and coffee at his desk. After saving one merchant’s business, he interrupted the man’s grateful blubbering to say, “No, it is a busy day. There’s no time for that. Good morning.”[53](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2209295) Few were privy to his thoughts, and he often had his own unstated agenda. Journalist Clarence W. Barron tells the story of a young Boston financier, F. H. Prince, who went to Pierpont for investment advice. Prince confessed, “I shook Mr. Morgan’s hand and thanked him warmly for the great interest he was taking in me as a young man and said I should never forget his advice. I knew at this time that he was doing everything he could to ruin me.”[54](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2209466)
皮尔庞特的故事里，不乏他待人粗率、不善言辞的桥段。他注意力难持久，有时只从上午十一点工作到下午三四点，中间在办公桌前吃个三明治、馅饼，喝杯咖啡便算午餐。某次救了一家商户的生意后，对方感激涕零，他却打断道：“不，今天很忙，没空听这些。早安。” [53](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2209295) 旁人鲜少能窥见他的想法，他常有自己的未言之谋。记者克拉伦斯·W·巴伦讲过一位年轻的波士顿金融家 F·H·普林斯的经历：普林斯去找皮尔庞特寻求投资建议，他坦言：“我握着摩根先生的手，对他对我这个年轻人的厚爱表示衷心感谢，并说我会永远铭记他的建议。而那时我已知道，他正竭尽全力要毁掉我。” [54](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2209466)

After Junius’s death, Pierpont needed to loosen his autocratic grip, as the sheer volume of work outgrew his need for domination. He had long bewailed his inability to delegate authority—“It is my nature and I cannot help it”—and held no formal meeting of his partners until after the 1907 panic.[55](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2209626) Despite the scope of his vision, Pierpont was extremely attentive to details and took pride in the knowledge that he could perform any job in the bank: “I can sit down at any clerk’s desk, take up his work where he left it and go on with it. . . . I don’t like being at any man’s mercy.”[56](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2209781) He never entirely renounced the founder’s itch to know the most minute details of the business. He examined the cash balance daily, boasted he could pay off all debts in two hours, had a deadeye for fake figures in scanning a ledger, and personally audited the books each New Year’s Day. When he found an error, the effect could be memorable for the responsible employee. “He was a perfectly huge man and he had a voice like a bull,” said Leonhard A. Keyes, then an office boy who wound the gold Tiffany clock on his desk.[57](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2209952)
朱尼厄斯去世后，皮尔庞特不得不放松他那独裁式的掌控，因为繁重的工作量已经超出了他对支配权的需求。他长期哀叹自己无法放权——“这是我的天性，我无能为力”——直到 1907 年恐慌之后，他才召集了首次正式合伙人会议。 [55](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2209626) 尽管视野宏阔，皮尔庞特对细节却极其敏锐，并为自己能胜任银行里任何一项工作而自豪：“我可以坐在任何职员的桌前，从他停下的地方接起工作继续干下去……我不喜欢受制于人。” [56](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2209781) 他从未完全放弃创始人那种要了解业务最细微环节的执念。他每日核查现金余额，吹嘘自己能在两小时内清偿所有债务，浏览账目时对虚假数字一眼看穿，而且每年元旦都亲自审计账本。一旦发现差错，相关员工往往会留下终身难忘的教训。 “他身材极其魁梧，嗓音像公牛一样洪亮。”莱昂哈德·A·凯斯说道，当时他还是个办公室勤杂工，负责给他桌上那座蒂芙尼金钟上发条。 [57](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2209952)

Pierpont Morgan’s power flourished during the steep industrial recession that began in 1893. Over fifteen thousand commercial firms failed in a contraction that led to class warfare and quasi-revolutionary strife in many parts of the United States. The bloody rout of steel workers in the Homestead strike of 1892 gave way to the government’s merciless crushing of the 1894 Pullman strike. Over six hundred banks failed during this period, and cash grew so scarce as a result of hoarding that brokers traded it on Wall Street curbs. Every company that failed and was reorganized by a bank ended up the bank’s captive client. In 1892, General Electric had been formed through a consolidation of the Edison General Electric Company and Thomson-Houston Electric. When the new company failed the next year, Pierpont rescued it and thus insured GE’s future loyalty to the House of Morgan.
皮尔庞特·摩根的权势在 1893 年开始的严重工业衰退中急剧膨胀。这场经济紧缩导致超过一万五千家商业公司倒闭，在美国多地引发了阶级对抗乃至近乎革命的动荡。1892 年霍姆斯特德罢工中钢铁工人的惨遭镇压，紧接着是 1894 年政府无情粉碎普尔曼罢工。这期间，六百多家银行破产，囤积现金导致资金极度匮乏，经纪商不得不在华尔街场外进行交易。每一家倒闭后被银行重组的企业，最终都沦为该银行的附庸客户。1892 年，通用电气通过爱迪生通用电气公司与汤姆逊-休斯顿电气公司的合并而成立。当这家新公司在次年陷入困境时，皮尔庞特出手相救，从而确保了通用电气日后对摩根财团的忠诚。

Oppressed by debt and overbuilding, more than a third of the country’s railway trackage fell into receivership, and English investors exhorted Pierpont to bring order to the industry. Thwarted by gentleman’s agreements, Pierpont now tried another approach to forming railway cartels: he would reorganize bankrupt roads and transfer control to himself. Then he wouldn’t be at the whim of government or feuding railway chiefs. In reorganizing railways, he ascended to a new plateau of power, beyond what any other private businessman had yet achieved. The lengthy catalogue of railroads that fell under his control included the Erie, Chesapeake and Ohio, Philadelphia and Reading, Santa Fe, Northern Pacific, Great Northern, New York Central, Lehigh Valley, Jersey Central, and the Southern Railway. Virtually every bankrupt road east of the Mississippi eventually passed through such reorganization, or morganization, as it was called. Some thirty-three thousand miles of railroad—one-sixth of the country’s trackage—were morganized. The companies’ combined revenues approached an amount equal to half of the U.S. government’s annual receipts.
在债务和过度建设的双重压力下，美国超过三分之一的铁路线陷入破产接管，英国投资者敦促皮尔庞特出面整顿行业。面对君子协定屡屡受阻，皮尔庞特转而采取新策略来组建铁路卡特尔：他重组破产的铁路线，并将控制权收归己有。这样一来，他便不再受制于政府或相互争斗的铁路巨头们。通过重组铁路，他的权势达到前所未有的新高度，超越了任何其他私人企业家。在他掌控之下的铁路名单颇为冗长，包括伊利铁路、切萨皮克与俄亥俄铁路、费城与雷丁铁路、圣塔菲铁路、北太平洋铁路、大北方铁路、纽约中央铁路、利哈伊谷铁路、泽西中央铁路以及南方铁路。密西西比河以东几乎所有破产的铁路线，最终都经历了这样的重组——或者说，像当时人们所称的“摩根式重组”。 大约三万三千英里的铁路——占全国铁路网的六分之一——被摩根化重组。这些公司的总收入接近美国政府年收入的一半。

It is hard to exaggerate the power that Pierpont accrued. Railroads then comprised 60 percent of all issues on the New York Stock Exchange. Utility and industrial stocks were rated as too speculative for insurance companies and savings institutions, putting railroads in a blue-chip category by themselves. Also, by issuing free passes to politicians, the railroads exercised a giant, corrupting influence on state legislatures. As his bank became a gigantic mill for bankrupt railroads, Pierpont routinely picked up \$l-million fees.
皮尔庞特积聚的权力之大，难以言喻。当时铁路股票占纽约证券交易所全部上市股票的 60%。公用事业和工业股票被认为风险过高，不适合保险公司和储蓄机构，使铁路股票成为唯一的蓝筹股。此外，铁路公司通过向政客发放免费通行证，对州立法机构施加了巨大而腐败的影响。随着他的银行变成破产铁路的巨型加工厂，皮尔庞特 routinely 收取 100 万美元的酬金。

With morganization, fixed railway costs were slimmed, and creditors were forced to swap their bonds for ones with lower interest rates, enabling roads to resume debt service. Pierpont would also put a lien on the railroads’ vast land and mineral holdings, so that money couldn’t be diverted to other enterprises. A court case nearly a hundred years later would show how binding these arrangements were. In 1987, the Burlington Northern Railroad tried to free itself from covenants Pierpont had imposed on the bonds of its predecessor, the Northern Pacific, which fell into receivership in 1893. He had put a lien on 1.9 million acres of land and 2.4 million acres of mineral rights, stipulating that all proceeds should go to improving the road. Analysts estimated that coal, oil, gas, and other minerals on the affected lands were worth billions of dollars. From beyond the grave, Pierpont stood up foursquare for creditors.
通过摩根化，铁路的固定成本被削减，债权人被迫将债券换成利率更低的债券，使铁路得以恢复偿债能力。皮尔庞特还会对铁路公司庞大的土地和矿产持有权设置留置权，从而防止资金被挪用至其他企业。近一百年后的一桩诉讼案揭示了这些安排有多么具有约束力。1987 年，伯灵顿北方铁路公司试图摆脱皮尔庞特对其前身——1893 年陷入破产管理的北太平洋铁路公司——债券所施加的契约条款。他曾对 190 万英亩土地和 240 万英亩矿产权利设置留置权，规定所有收益必须用于改善铁路。分析师估计，受影响土地上蕴含的煤炭、石油、天然气及其他矿产价值数十亿美元。即便在身后，皮尔庞特依然坚定地捍卫债权人利益。

As a further guarantee that the roads would never again squander money, a majority of their stock was transferred to “voting trusts.” These were usually a euphemism for Pierpont and three or four of his cronies, who ran the railroads, typically for a five-year period. It was an extension of Pierpont’s old trick of trading money for power, and it usurped commercial power on a scale unprecedented in banking history. No longer would the banker just finance and advise his clients; now he would intervene directly in running the companies. The distinction between finance and industry was eroding dangerously.
为确保铁路不再挥霍资金，其大部分股票被转移至“投票信托”。这通常是皮尔庞特与他三四个亲信的代名词，他们负责经营铁路，任期通常为五年。这是皮尔庞特以钱换权的老把戏的延伸，并以银行史上前所未有的规模篡夺了商业权力。银行家不再仅仅为客户提供融资和建议，而是直接干预公司运营。金融与工业之间的界限正危险地模糊。

Why would tens of thousands of shareholders yield their shares to this Wall Street pope in exchange for so-called trust certificates? The answer lies in a peculiarity of nineteenth-century finance: when companies lost money, shareholders in bankrupted companies could be dunned for assessments. So investors rushed to give up their shares and avoid the threatened penalties. Pierpont was now an altogether new species of robber baron—not nakedly voracious, not a Rockefeller snuffing out troublesome competitors, but a gruff, well-tailored banker with a legal, if highly controversial, system.
为何数以万计的股东甘愿将手中股份拱手让与这位华尔街教皇，换取所谓的信托凭证？答案藏在 19 世纪金融的一个奇特现象中：公司亏损时，破产企业的股东可能被追缴额外款项。于是投资者们争相抛售股份，以躲避这笔飞来横债。彼时，皮尔庞特已蜕变为一个全新物种的强盗大亨——既非赤裸裸的饕餮之徒，也非洛克菲勒那样铲除异己的狠角色，而是一位穿着考究、态度生硬的银行家，手持一套合法却备受争议的金融体系。

Within the bank, morganization was viewed benignly as the exercise of fiduciary responsibility to shareholders. Pierpont didn’t seem to operate by any grand scheme—he was too instinctive for that. A later Morgan partner, Tom Lamont, remarked that he “never knew of a man who addressed himself more exclusively than Mr. Morgan to the ad hoc situation and the ad hoc job that lay before him. All this talk about his devising or building up systems is perfect tosh.”[58](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2210105) Pierpont didn’t spin webs or plot paths to power. Rather, he had a messianic faith in his ability to reorder businesses. If he could tidy up America better than anyone else, so be it. He took the technique of the voting trust and endlessly multiplied his power. As Sereno S. Pratt, an editor of the *Wall Street Journal*, later said of him, “His power is not to be found in the number of his own millions, but in the billions of which he was the trustee.”[59](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2210301)
在银行内部，摩根的行为被善意地视为对股东履行受托责任。皮尔庞特似乎并不遵循什么宏大计划——他太凭直觉行事了。后来的摩根合伙人汤姆·拉蒙特评价道：“我从未见过像摩根先生那样更专注于眼前具体事务和具体工作的人。所有那些关于他设计或构建体系的说法，纯粹是胡扯。” [58](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2210105) 皮尔庞特并不编织关系网，也不谋划权力之路。相反，他对自己重组企业的能力怀有弥赛亚般的信念。如果他比任何人都更能把美国理清，那便如此吧。他运用股权信托的技巧，不断放大自己的权力。正如《华尔街日报》编辑塞雷诺·S·普拉特后来评价他的那样：“他的权力不在于他自己拥有多少百万财富，而在于他是数十亿美元财富的受托人。” [59](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2210301)

If there was nothing devious about the voting trusts, they created a frightening concentration of Wall Street power. Before the morganization period, more than two-thirds of American railroads had offices outside New York; afterward, most were headquartered there. By 1900, the nation’s railroads were consolidated into six huge systems controlled by Wall Street bankers, principally J. P. Morgan and Company and Kuhn, Loeb. In this perpetual-motion machine, Pierpont not only reorganized roads but locked up their future financing. By acting as their trustee or holding a large block of their stock, he ensured bondage to 23 Wall. The banker was strong because the railroads were weak, and however much Pierpont deplored railroad instability, he thrived on such chaos.
如果说投票信托本身并无险恶之处，那么它们确实造就了华尔街权力令人恐惧的集中。在摩根化时期之前，美国超过三分之二的铁路公司总部设在纽约以外；此后，大部分铁路公司都将总部迁至纽约。到 1900 年，全国铁路系统整合为六大巨头，由华尔街银行家掌控，主要是 J.P.摩根公司以及库恩-洛布公司。在这台永动机中，皮尔庞特不仅重组铁路线路，还牢牢锁定了它们未来的融资渠道。通过担任受托人或持有大量股票，他确保这些公司对 23 号华尔街俯首听命。银行家的强大源于铁路的脆弱，尽管皮尔庞特对铁路行业的动荡深感不满，但他却正是靠这种混乱大发其财。

Pierpont alone could never have carried out the exhausting work of morganization. Hence the importance, then and later, of Morgan partners. In history books, they are often portrayed as mice scurrying in the background. Yet many were towering figures in their own right, the shadow cabinet of the Morgan government. The railroad reorganizations were carried out by a staff of fewer than 150 employees. This was at a time when old-fashioned banks, such as the House of Morgan, frowned upon typewriters as newfangled. Visitors always marveled at the discrepancy between the bank’s power and its size. In 1905, Dr. Hjalmar Schacht, later Hitler’s finance minister, recorded this impression: “The entire office was contained in a single room on the ground floor in which were dozens of desks where the employees worked. . . . No question of visitors being formally announced, no waiting, or anterooms. Anyone who saw that a principal was disengaged could walk right up to his desk. Relations between heads and employees were very informal and free-and-easy without thereby lacking in respect.”[60](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2210456)
单靠皮尔庞特一人，绝无可能完成那项繁重无比的摩根化工作。因此，无论当时还是日后，摩根合伙人的重要性都不言而喻。历史书籍中，他们往往被描绘成在幕后忙碌穿梭的老鼠。然而，许多人本身便是举足轻重的人物，堪称摩根政府下的影子内阁。铁路重组工作由一支不足 150 人的团队完成。而彼时，像摩根银行这样守旧的机构，甚至对打字机这种新潮玩意嗤之以鼻。访客们无不惊叹于这家银行所拥有的滔天权势与它那袖珍规模之间的巨大反差。1905 年，后来成为希特勒财政部长的亚尔马·沙赫特博士记下了这样的印象：“整个办公室仅设在一楼的一个房间里，里面摆着几十张办公桌，雇员们就在那里工作……没有正式的访客通报，没有等候，也没有接待室。任何人只要看到某位负责人有空，就可以径直走到他的桌前。” 领导与员工之间的关系非常不拘礼节、自由自在，但又不失尊重。 [60](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2210456)

Pierpont selected partners not by wealth or to fortify the bank’s capital but based on brains and talent. If the Morgan style was royal, its hiring practices were meritocratic. The bank had many first-rate technicians. Pierpont’s transportation man, Samuel Spencer, was said to know better than anyone in America every detail of railroading “from the cost of a car brake to the estimate for a terminal.”[61](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2210642) Most impressive was Charles Coster, a pale man with neatly brushed hair, pensive eyes, and handlebar mustache. As a young man, Coster had published a history of stamps, and his compulsion to organize and classify never left him. He was the obscure wizard of morganization. Jack Morgan said of him, “His mastery of detail was complete, his grasp of a problem immediate and comprehensive and his power of work astonishing.”[62](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2210797) Wall Street caught fleeting glances of this sedentary genius: “Men saw him by day—a white-faced, nervous figure, hurrying from directors’ meeting to directors’ meeting; at evening carrying home his portfolio of corporate problems for the night.”[63](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2210931) Yet Coster was no downtrodden clerk: thanks to the wonders of voting trusts, he sat on the boards of fifty-nine corporations\!
皮尔庞特选择合伙人不是看财富，也不是为了充实银行资本，而是基于才智和天赋。如果说摩根风格是皇家气派，那么它的用人原则则是唯才是举。银行里有许多一流的技术专家。皮尔庞特的运输业务主管塞缪尔·斯宾塞据说比美国任何人都更了解铁路的每一个细节——"从一节车厢的制动成本到一座终点站的造价估算"。 [61](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2210642) 最令人印象深刻的是查尔斯·科斯特，他面色苍白，头发梳理得一丝不苟，目光深沉，留着八字胡。年轻时，科斯特曾出版过一本邮票史，他对整理与分类的执念从未消退。他是摩根式重组中默默无闻的奇才。杰克·摩根这样评价他："他对细节的掌握无懈可击，对问题的理解既迅速又全面，工作能力令人惊叹。"” [62](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2210797) Wall Street 偶尔瞥见这位深居简出的天才：“白天人们看到他——一个面色苍白、神情紧张的身影，匆匆从一个董事会赶往另一个董事会；晚上则带着一摞公司问题的卷宗回家过夜。” [63](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2210931) 然而，Coster 并非一个受压迫的小职员：多亏了投票信托的奇迹，他同时担任着五十九家公司的董事！

The House of Morgan would have a contradictory reputation as both a gentleman’s club and a posh sweatshop. During the morganization period, lights burned at the bank long after the rest of Wall Street was dark. The partners shouldered unbearable tasks. One journalist remarked that “the House of Morgan was always known as a partner-killer,” and the body count mounted steadily. One day in 1894, while waiting for an elevated train after the business day, partner J. Hood Wright dropped dead at the age of fifty-eight. The most shocking death was Coster’s, in March 1900, at age forty-eight. He contracted flu or pneumonia and died within a week. Mixing sympathy with outrage, the *New York Times* charged that the tasks piled upon Coster had grown “far heavier than any one man ought to bear, or could bear with safety.” Naming Morgan partners who died from overwork by 1900, John Moody said they had “succumbed to the gigantic, nerve-racking business and pressure of the Morgan methods and the strain involved in the care of the railroad capital of America. ’Jupiter’ Morgan had alone come through that soul-crushing mill of business, retaining his health, vigor, and energy.”[64](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2211101)
摩根财团的名声自相矛盾，既是绅士俱乐部，又是豪华血汗工厂。在摩根化时期，当华尔街其他地区早已灯火阑珊时，摩根银行的灯光依然通明。合伙人们承担着难以负荷的重任。一位记者评论道："摩根财团向来以'合伙人杀手'著称"，而死亡名单也在不断增加。1894 年的一天，合伙人 J.胡德·赖特在结束一天的工作后等待高架列车时，突然倒地身亡，年仅五十八岁。最令人震惊的死亡发生在 1900 年 3 月，合伙人科斯特年仅四十八岁。他感染了流感或肺炎，一周内便撒手人寰。《纽约时报》既同情又愤慨地指责道，堆积在科斯特身上的任务"远超任何一个人所能承受或安全承担的极限"。约翰·穆迪列举了到 1900 年因过劳而离世的摩根合伙人，称他们“不堪重负，倒在了摩根那庞大而令人神经紧绷的业务模式与压力之下，以及管理美国铁路资本所带来的劳神费心之中。唯有‘朱庇特’摩根挺过了那磨灭灵魂的商业熔炉，依旧保持着健康、活力与干劲。” [64](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2211101)

In choosing partners, Pierpont wouldn’t tolerate a refusal. He was shameless enough to recruit Coster’s successor, railroad lawyer Charles Steele, at Coster’s funeral! As the cortege moved along, Pierpont presented a partnership to Steele as a *fait accompli.* “Charles,” he said, “it looks as if the Lord had taken charge of this question, and I am going ahead to make the partnership agreement.”[65](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2211264) The courtly Steele later accumulated thirty-six corporate directorships, including those of United States Steel and General Electric, and his wealth would rival Jack Morgan’s.
在选择合伙人时，皮尔庞特容不得半点拒绝。他甚至无耻到在科斯特的葬礼上招募其继任者——铁路律师查尔斯·斯蒂尔！送葬队伍缓缓前行之际，皮尔庞特便把合伙协议当成了既成事实强加给斯蒂尔。“查尔斯，”他说，“看来上帝已经接手了这个问题，我这就去拟定合伙协议了。” [65](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2211264) 这位温文尔雅的斯蒂尔后来累计担任了三十六家公司的董事，其中包括美国钢铁公司和通用电气，其财富足以与杰克·摩根相匹敌。

Even as the exhausting pace of work created scandals, a Morgan partnership became the most coveted financial post. Judge Elbert H. Gary, a chairman of United States Steel, said of Pierpont’s partners, “He made them all wealthy beyond their dreams.”[66](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2211434) Indeed, in exchange for exquisite torture, a Morgan partner received a guarantee of riches and a seat on the high council of American finance.
尽管繁重的工作节奏引发了丑闻，但摩根合伙人的职位仍成为金融界最令人垂涎的职位。美国钢铁公司董事长埃尔伯特·H·加里法官在谈到皮尔庞特的合伙人时说道：“他让他们所有人都富甲一方，远超梦想。” [66](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_048.html_filepos2211434) 的确，作为对极尽磨难的交换，摩根合伙人换来了财富的保障和美国金融界最高层的一席之地。
