# Chapter Twenty-Four: Passages

THE early war years saw the final transformation of J. P. Morgan and Company from a private partnership to a corporation. This momentous step in Morgan history was taken only after extensive deliberations at the Pierpont Morgan Library. In announcing the transformation in February 1940, Jack made an unprecedented appearance at the press conference. He would be board chairman, George Whitney chief executive, and Lamont head of the executive committee. In dropping the partnership form, Jack had to sell the New York Stock Exchange seat bought by Pierpont in 1895. As a private bank, partners had been exposed to the full risk of loss. But they had gladly accepted this risk in order to keep their capital position secret and their books free from inspection. This tradition had contributed immeasurably to the firm’s mystery and power.
二战初期，J. P. 摩根公司完成了从私人合伙制向股份制公司的最终转型。摩根历史上这一重大举措，是在皮尔庞特·摩根图书馆经过深思熟虑后才付诸实施的。1940 年 2 月宣布转型时，杰克破天荒地亲自出席了新闻发布会。他将出任董事会主席，乔治·惠特尼担任首席执行官，拉蒙特则执掌执行委员会。随着合伙制的终结，杰克不得不卖掉皮尔庞特于 1895 年购入的纽约证券交易所席位。作为私人银行，合伙人曾需承担全部亏损风险，但他们欣然接受这一代价——为的是保密资本状况，使账簿免受外界审查。这一传统为这家公司平添了难以估量的神秘感与实力，功不可没。

Why, then, the change? The bank feared rapid capital depletion as the three richest partners aged: Tom Lamont, Charles Steele, and Jack Morgan. Steele had died in Westbury, New York, in mid-1939, after spending his last years watching his grandsons play polo. If either Jack Morgan or Lamont died soon, too, there could be a serious drain on capital. A combination of the Depression and inheritance and income taxes had whittled the bank’s assets down from \$ 119 million in 1929 to only \$39 million in 1940. By converting to share ownership, heirs could sell their stakes without disrupting the bank’s capital. There was also a wish to enter the trust business, which was closed to partnerships. In 1927, American Telephone and Telegraph had funded the first big corporate retirement plan, and Morgans wanted to capture similar huge pools of capital.
那么，为何要转变呢？银行担心随着三位最富有的合伙人——Tom Lamont、Charles Steele 和 Jack Morgan 年事渐高，资本会快速流失。Steele 于 1939 年年中在纽约州韦斯特伯里去世，临终前几年他都在观看孙子们打马球。如果 Jack Morgan 或 Lamont 也很快离世，资本将面临严重流失。大萧条加上遗产税和所得税的冲击，使银行资产从 1929 年的 1.19 亿美元锐减至 1940 年的仅 3900 万美元。通过转为股份所有制，继承人可以出售所持股份，而不会影响银行的资本。此外，银行也希望涉足信托业务——合伙制企业无法涉足这一领域。1927 年，美国电话电报公司（American Telephone and Telegraph）资助了首个大型企业退休计划，而 Morgans 家族意图抓住类似规模的巨额资金池。

There were other blows to Morgans’ traditional aloofness as well. In 1942, it joined the Federal Reserve System—having been the largest holdout—in a move related to its heavy purchase of government bonds, which was Wall Street’s principal wartime activity: the Corner witnessed Victory Loan rallies for which huge throngs turned out before a flag-draped Stock Exchange. Now for the first time, the House of Morgan’s nearly \$700 million in deposits became subject to federal deposit insurance. Also in 1942, ownership of Morgan shares spread beyond the eighty or ninety people, mostly family and friends, who had controlled it before. A syndicate led by Smith, Barney offered 8 percent of Morgan shares to the public—the first time ordinary mortals could buy a piece of a Morgan bank. This both broadened ownership and assigned value to closely held shares. In a final affront to tradition, J. P. Morgan and Company disclosed its earnings in a prospectus.
此外，摩根家族传统的孤高姿态还遭受了其他打击。1942 年，摩根公司加入了美联储体系——此前它一直是最大的抵制者——这一举措与其大量购买政府债券有关，而购买政府债券是华尔街在战时的主要活动。华尔街角见证了胜利公债集会，当时在挂满旗帜的证券交易所前，聚集了庞大的人群。如今，摩根家族近 7 亿美元的存款首次受到联邦存款保险的覆盖。同样在 1942 年，摩根股份的所有权扩展到了之前掌控它的八九十人（主要是家族成员和朋友）之外。由 Smith, Barney 领导的银团向公众提供了摩根 8%的股份——这是普通人首次能够购买摩根银行的股份。这既扩大了所有权，也赋予了紧密持有的股份以价值。作为对传统的最后一项挑战，J. P. Morgan and Company 在一份招股说明书中披露了其收益。

In this period of transition, the Morgan link with its Philadelphia affiliate, Drexel and Company, also ended. The Philadelphia firm had brought the Drexels, Biddies, Berwinds, and other Main Line families into the Morgan fold. As Pierpont had told Arsene Pujo, “It only has a different name, owing to my desire to keep Mr. Drexel’s name in Philadelphia.”[1](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2377998) In 1940, 23 Wall took over Drexel’s deposits, shut the Philadelphia office, and sold the name to some Philadelphia partners who were forming an investment bank. Later on, I. W. “Tubby” Burn-ham merged his Burnham and Company with the reincarnated Drexel, so that the famous name would later grace the junk-bond operation of Drexel Burnham Lambert.
在这个过渡时期，摩根公司与费城联营公司 Drexel and Company 的关系也画上了句号。这家费城公司曾将 Drexel 家族、Biddle 家族、Berwind 家族以及其他 Main Line 名门望族引入摩根体系。正如皮尔庞特对 Arsene Pujo 所说：“这只是换个名字而已，因为我希望 Mr. Drexel 的名字在费城得以保留。” [1](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2377998) 1940 年，华尔街 23 号接管了 Drexel 的存款，关闭了费城办事处，并将这个名字卖给了一些正在组建投资银行的费城合伙人。后来，I. W. “Tubby” Burnham 将其 Burnham and Company 与复活的 Drexel 合并，使得这个响亮的名字后来成为了 Drexel Burnham Lambert 垃圾债券业务的荣耀。

To qualify for Stock Exchange membership, Morgan Stanley became a partnership in 1941. It was now harried by the Brandeisian trustbusters who had pursued J. P. Morgan and Company and saw Morgan Stanley as simply a retooled version of the original company. Morgan Stanley’s instant success had aroused suspicion, for it had managed a quarter of all negotiated bond issues since Glass-Steagall. During the Temporary National Economic Committee hearings in 1939 and 1940, the committee’s chairman, Senator Joseph O’Mahoney of Wyoming, refused to believe J. P. Morgan had withdrawn from investment banking: “Now that the Banking Act has separated two functions that were formerly merged, Morgan Stanley in the investment field has succeeded to a similar dominant position that J.P. Morgan formerly held.”[2](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2378227) SEC counsel John Hauser advanced a conspiracy theory that dismissed Morgan Stanley as a “legal fiction” set up by J. P. Morgan partners to bypass Glass-Steagall. An exasperated Harold Stanley was repeatedly asked whether he took orders from 23 Wall after Glass-Steagall. “We were a separate, split-off organization,” he insisted. “We owned and ran the business. Our money had been risked in the common stock.”[3](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2378467) Notwithstanding his denials, the SEC charged that J. P. Morgan and Company had used its influence over Dayton Power to obtain business for Morgan Stanley.
为了获得证券交易所会员资格，摩根士丹利于 1941 年转型为合伙制企业。此时它正受到布兰代斯派反垄断者的困扰——这些人当年曾追查 J.P.摩根公司，并认定摩根士丹利不过是原公司的翻版。摩根士丹利的迅速成功引发了猜疑，因为自《格拉斯-斯蒂格尔法案》实施以来，它已承揽了四分之一的有价证券协商发行。在 1939 年至 1940 年的临时国家经济委员会听证会上，委员会主席、怀俄明州参议员约瑟夫·奥马奥尼拒绝相信 J.P.摩根已退出投资银行业务：“既然《银行法》将原本合并的两种职能分离开来，摩根士丹利在投资领域已成功继承了 J.P.摩根原先占据的类似主导地位。” [2](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2378227) 美国证券交易委员会律师约翰·豪泽则抛出了阴谋论，将摩根士丹利贬斥为 J.P.摩根合伙人为了规避《格拉斯-斯蒂格尔法案》而设立的“法律虚构实体”。 恼怒的哈罗德·斯坦利（Harold Stanley）在《格拉斯-斯蒂格尔法案》通过后，一再被问及他是否听命于华尔街 23 号。“我们是独立、分离出来的组织，”他坚持道，“我们拥有并经营自己的业务。我们的资金都投在了普通股上，承担着风险。” [3](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2378467) 尽管他否认，SEC 仍指控 J. P. Morgan and Company 利用其对 Dayton Power 的影响力，为 Morgan Stanley 争取业务。

What weakened Morgan Stanley’s claim to autonomy was that most of its preferred stock was owned by J. P. Morgan and Company officers. The SEC asserted this created “an identity of pecuniary interest” between the two Morgan houses and an “emotional” and “psychological” affiliation.[4](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2378618) So Morgan Stanley began buying the preferred stock, and J. P. Morgan executives sold it to their wives, sons, grandchildren, and so forth—a transparent ruse that didn’t fool anybody. To retire the bogey of J. P. Morgan control once and for all, Morgan Stanley redeemed and canceled its preferred shares on December 5, 1941. This ended any formal link between the two firms, although a multitude of intangible links would weave them together for decades.
削弱摩根士丹利对独立性主张的关键在于，其大部分优先股由 J.P.摩根公司的高管持有。美国证券交易委员会指出，这导致两家摩根机构之间形成了"利益共同体"，并存在"情感"与"心理"层面的依附关系。 [4](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2378618) 于是摩根士丹利开始回购优先股，而 J.P.摩根的高管们则将股份转售给自己的妻子、子女、孙辈等人——这种显而易见的伎俩骗不了任何人。为彻底消除 J.P.摩根控制权的阴影，摩根士丹利于 1941 年 12 月 5 日赎回了全部优先股并予以注销。此举终结了两家公司之间的任何正式关联，尽管此后数十年间仍有无数无形纽带将二者紧密相连。

At this point, the campaign against Wall Street shifted to a lower gear. The investment banking business was moribund during the war as the Treasury Department asked underwriters to desist from new bond issues so as not to compete with government war-bond drives. Therefore, the drive to reform investment banking was stalled until the Medina trial of the early postwar years. In the meantime, by switching to a partnership form, Morgan Stanley retreated into the world of “mystery and dignity,” as Judge Medina later labeled it, just as J. P. Morgan was emerging into the sunlight.
此时，针对华尔街的攻势暂缓。战争期间，投资银行业务几乎停滞，财政部要求承销商暂停新债发行，以免与政府战争债券发行竞争。因此，投资银行改革进程中断，直到战后初期的梅迪纳审判才得以重启。在此期间，摩根士丹利转为合伙制，退入了后来梅迪纳法官所称的“神秘与尊严”的世界，而与此同时，J. P. 摩根正逐渐走向阳光之下。

AFTER initial grumbling, Jack Morgan settled amiably into his new role as board chairman. “What he had looked forward to with distaste he found not at all disagreeable,” said Russell Leffingwell.[5](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2378785) On January 31, 1943, Jack presided over the first public shareholders meeting of J. P. Morgan and Company, Inc. It was a pleasant, autumnal time for him: the war had silenced New Deal charges of villainy, and everybody was saying that Jack hadn’t seemed so happy since before Jessie’s death, eighteen years before. He enjoyed serving as nanny to his English war babies and went duck shooting nearly every weekend that fall. There were gentler pursuits as well, including the new hobby of taking color photographs of cherry blossoms and other flowers.
起初杰克的抱怨渐渐平息，他随和地接受了董事会主席的新角色。“他原本厌恶的事，做起来却一点也不讨厌，”拉塞尔·莱芬韦尔说道。1943 年 1 月 31 日，杰克主持了 J.P.摩根公司首次公开股东大会。对他而言，这是一段怡人的秋日时光：战争让新政指控他作恶的声浪沉寂，大家都说杰克自十八年前杰西去世后，从未如此开心过。他乐于充当英国“战争婴儿”的保姆，那年秋天几乎每个周末都去打野鸭。此外还有更文雅的消遣，比如用彩色摄影拍樱花和其他花卉的新爱好。

The soft-shoe, avuncular Jack was much more in evidence. Every evening, he stopped to chat with the Pinkerton guards at Matinicock Point, thanking them as they opened the gates that guarded the estate. Playing backgammon with John Davis for a nickel a game, he had a winning streak and teased Davis’s butler that he was about to lose his wages. He observed life’s smaller details. Each morning, at the same bend in the road, he passed a young neighbor driving to work in the opposite direction; when the young man overslept one morning and they passed further down the road than usual, Jack wagged a satirical finger of rebuke.
穿着软底鞋、慈祥的杰克更常露面了。每晚，他都会在马蒂尼科克角停下来，和保安公司的警卫聊上几句，感谢他们打开庄园的大门。他和约翰·戴维斯玩双陆棋，一局五分钱，赢了一连串，还打趣戴维斯的管家说他要输掉薪水了。他留意生活中的小细节。每天早晨，在同一条路的同一处弯道，他会遇到一位年轻的邻居开车去上班，方向相反；有天早上年轻人睡过了头，他们在比平时更远的路段相遇，杰克便戏谑地竖起了手指以示责备。

In late February, doctors gave Jack a clean bill of health before he left for a Florida holiday, a quiet fishing trip on the Gulf of Mexico. On the train to Boca Grande, he had heart trouble, however, followed by a cerebral stroke. His steadfast valet of twenty-eight years, Bernard Stewart, managed to get him to his rented cottage at the Gasparilla Inn, a winter resort on a barrier island, and his New York heart specialist, Dr. Henry S. Patterson, came down to look after him. Jack survived less than two weeks. He died in a coma on March 13, 1943, and his body was brought north in a special Pullman car attached to the Seaboard Line.
2 月下旬，医生给杰克开具了健康证明，随后他前往佛罗里达度假，到墨西哥湾进行一趟安静的垂钓之旅。然而，在前往博卡格兰德的火车上，他突发心脏问题，随后又发生脑卒中。他忠实的贴身男仆伯纳德·斯图尔特（为他服务 28 年）设法将他送到位于加斯帕里拉旅馆的租住别墅——那是一家位于障壁岛上的冬季度假胜地。杰克在纽约的心脏病专家亨利·S·帕特森医生也赶来照料他。杰克没能撑过两周。1943 年 3 月 13 日，他在昏迷中去世，他的遗体被安置在海岸线铁路专用普尔曼车厢中运回北方。

Even in death, there were eerie parallels between Jack and Pierpont Morgan. Both died at the age of seventy-five, and again news of the death was withheld until after the stock market closed, so as not to disturb share prices. The voluminous obituaries that followed were of the full-page variety reserved for heads of state. The *New York Times* commented, “The private banking house of J.P. Morgan & Co. . . . gained a position of world-wide importance and a place in international financial affairs that not even the house of Rothschild attained in the period of its greatest power.”[6](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2378998) The paper called Jack the last financial titan—they had said the same thing of Pierpont—noting that for the first time since George Peabody’s retirement, the Morgan bank was not headed by a Morgan. Tom Lamont ascended to board chairman.
即便在死亡之际，杰克与皮尔庞特·摩根之间也存在着诡异的相似之处。两人都在七十五岁离世，死讯同样被封锁到股市收盘后才公布，以免影响股价。后续的讣告篇幅冗长，占据整版，规格堪比国家元首。《纽约时报》评论道：“J.P. Morgan & Co. 这家私人银行……赢得全球性的重要地位，并在国际金融事务中占据了一席之地，就连鼎盛时期的罗斯柴尔德家族也未能企及。” [6](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2378998) 该报称杰克为最后一位金融巨头——当年他们也曾用同样的话形容皮尔庞特——并指出，自乔治·皮博迪退休以来，摩根银行首次不由摩根家族成员执掌。汤姆·拉蒙特升任董事会主席。

Jack’s funeral service, too, was reminiscent of Pierpont’s. He lay in state at the Pierpont Morgan Library before a funeral at Saint George’s Church on Stuyvesant Square. The service featured the black baritone Harry Burleigh, who had sung at the 1913 funeral. Again flags flew at half-mast over the New York Stock Exchange and the Corner. One difference was subtly apparent to the twelve hundred mourners who arrived in a heavy downpour: they were solemnly escorted to their seats by the directors of *two* banking houses—J. P. Morgan and Company and Morgan Stanley. After cremation, Jack’s ashes were sent to Hartford for burial at Cedar Hill Cemetery, alongside the graves of Pierpont and Junius.
杰克的葬礼也让人联想到皮尔蓬特。他的遗体停放在皮尔蓬特·摩根图书馆，随后在斯图文森广场的圣乔治教堂举行了葬礼。仪式上，曾为 1913 年葬礼献唱的黑人男中音哈里·伯利再次登台。纽约证券交易所和华尔街角落再次降下半旗。对于冒雨赶来的 1200 名吊唁者而言，一个细微差别悄然显现：两家银行——J.P.摩根公司与摩根士丹利的董事们庄重地引领他们就座。火化后，杰克的骨灰被送往哈特福德，安葬在雪松山公墓，与皮尔蓬特和朱尼厄斯的墓地为邻。

In his will, Jack perpetuated Pierpont’s tradition of flamboyant generosity, including a \$ 1-million trust fund for his elderly domestic employees. Henry Physick, Jack’s butler of thirty-four years and the man who was so resourceful during the 1915 assassination attempt, received \$25; 000. His secretary of forty years, John Axten, hired as a boy of nineteen, got \$50,000, as did Belle da Costa Greene. With a paternalistic flourish in the style of Pierpont, Jack gave six months’ wages to long-time bank employees and three months’ to those hired more recently.
杰克在遗嘱中延续了皮尔庞特慷慨张扬的传统，包括为年迈的家佣设立了 100 万美元的信托基金。跟随他 34 年的管家亨利·菲西克——正是在 1915 年暗杀事件中足智多谋的那位——获得了 2.5 万美元。服务四十年的秘书约翰·阿克顿，自 19 岁起被雇佣，获得了 5 万美元；同样获此金额的还有贝尔·达·科斯塔·格林。杰克以皮尔庞特式的父权姿态，给予长期银行员工六个月薪水的遣散费，而入职较晚者则获得三个月薪水。

As they had been at his father’s death, everybody was surprised by the relative modesty of Jack’s estate—only \$16 million before taxes and expenses, \$4.6 million afterward. Following merchant-banking tradition, he left the bulk of his estate to his sons, Junius and Harry. His daughters’ families, the Nichols and the Pennoyers, would enjoy the prestige but less of the fortune associated with the Morgan name. During his lifetime, Jack gave away an estimated \$35 million, including \$ 15 million to the Pierpont Morgan Library and \$9 million to the Metropolitan Museum of Art. His fortune wasn’t frittered away only by philanthropy. After Jessie’s death, he had maintained the fantastic indulgence of the colossal yachts and the regal estates.
正如他父亲去世时那样，众人对杰克相对微薄的遗产感到意外——税前及开支前仅 1600 万美元，税后则为 460 万美元。遵循商人银行传统，他将大部分遗产留给了儿子朱尼厄斯和哈里。女儿们的家族，尼科尔斯家和彭诺耶家，虽能享有摩根姓氏带来的声望，但所得的财富却少得多。杰克生前捐赠了约 3500 万美元，其中 1500 万美元给了皮尔庞特·摩根图书馆，900 万美元给了大都会艺术博物馆。他的财富并非仅因慈善而散尽。在杰西去世后，他依然维持着对巨型游艇和豪华庄园的极致放纵。

Opinion of Jack’s place in history was immediately divided. Clearly, his business career had been a personal triumph. When he took over the bank, Wall Street rumor mills had patronized him as a bungler. Yet under him, the House of Morgan had amassed power beyond that of the bank under Junius or even under Pierpont. It had taken on extraordinary international breadth, winning many governments, finance ministries, and central banks as clients and capitalizing on the merger of politics and finance in the Diplomatic Age. The building at 23 Wall now seemed less a smoky clubhouse of banking cronies than a gathering place for the world’s financial elite. With some glaring exceptions—such as the Van Sweringen escapade and the Richard Whitney scandal—Jack had preserved the bank’s reputation for fair, conservative dealings.
世人对杰克历史地位的看法从一开始就褒贬不一。显然，他的职业生涯堪称个人胜利。他接手银行时，华尔街的谣言作坊曾把他当作一个笨手笨脚的新手。然而在他治下，摩根财团积聚的权力超越了朱尼厄斯乃至皮尔庞特时代。这家银行展现出非凡的国际视野，将众多政府、财政部和央行纳入客户名单，并在外交时代巧妙把握了政治与金融的融合。如今，华尔街 23 号那座烟雾缭绕的银行老友俱乐部，更像是全球金融精英的聚集地。尽管存在范斯韦林根丑闻和理查德·惠特尼丑闻等刺眼的败笔，但杰克大体上维护了银行公平、稳健经营的名声。

He had also put in place a superlative team and allowed its members to employ the full scope of their energies. He was a good “successor” figure who knew how to delegate power and take disinterested pleasure in his partners’ feats. If the Morgan bank moved like a well-oiled machine and was free of internal warfare, it had something to do with Jack’s reputable stewardship. A more self-centered boss might have regretted his own absence during the 1929 crash, yet Jack took fatherly pride in his partners’ behavior: “I . . . was made very happy by the absolutely magnificent conduct of all my partners during the ’late unpleasantness’ in Wall Street. The Firm showed that it could behave just as well when I was not there, as it could have done had I been there.”[7](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2379147) Unlike his father, he was never a prisoner of his ego.
他组建了一支卓越的团队，并允许成员们充分发挥全部能量。他是一位出色的"接班人"，懂得如何下放权力，并能从合作伙伴的成就中获得无私的喜悦。如果说摩根银行像一台运转顺畅的机器且没有内耗，那与杰克值得称道的管理密不可分。换作更以自我为中心的老板，或许会为 1929 年崩盘时自己不在场而遗憾，但杰克却以父亲般的自豪看待伙伴们的表现："...华尔街那段'不太愉快'的时期，我所有伙伴的绝对卓越表现让我无比欣慰。这家公司证明，即便我不在，它也能像我在时一样运转良好。" [7](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2379147) 与父亲不同，他从未被自我意识所囚禁。

Of Jack’s public role, a far less flattering judgment must be rendered. The *New Republic* acidly observed that Jack had “added nothing creative or humanizing to American life, and . . . his passing subtracts nothing.”[8](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2379310) In the Victorian age, he would have been a model banker, cherishing honor, integrity, and Christianity. Such values, however, were inadequate during the worldwide Depression, when many people went hungry while still abiding by them. It was a harsh Providence that dropped such a hidebound, frightened man into an age of radical upheaval and experimentation. He asked for privacy in an era that demanded accountability. Increasingly the Morgan bank operated as an adjunct of government. It couldn’t accept the benefits of public service without also accepting its burdens. Fleeing his political troubles, Jack kept aloof from his countrymen and never understood plain Americans the way he did English aristocrats. The *New Yorker* once said with justice, “One feels he could both teach and learn if he would cross the Mississippi frequently and meet the people that largely make up America.”[9](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2379444)
对于杰克在公共领域的角色，我们不得不给出一个远非褒扬的评价。《新共和》杂志尖刻地指出，杰克“既未给美国生活增添任何创意或人性化的东西，……他的离去也没有减损什么。” [8](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2379310) 在维多利亚时代，他本可以成为一位模范银行家，珍视荣誉、正直与基督教信仰。然而，面对全球性大萧条，当无数人忍饥挨饿却仍恪守这些价值观时，它们已显得苍白无力。命运何其残酷，竟将这样一个墨守成规、胆小怕事的人抛入一个激荡变革与实验的时代。他在一个要求问责的时代里乞求隐私。摩根银行日渐沦为政府的附庸。它无法只享受公共服务之利，却拒绝承受其重负。为逃避政治麻烦，杰克与他的同胞们保持距离，从未像理解英国贵族那样理解普通的美国人。 《纽约客》曾一语中的地指出：“要是他能多跨过密西西比河，去接触那些真正构成美国主体的人们，他一定会觉得自己既能教，也能学。”” [9](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2379444)

At a time that demanded fresh thinking, Jack could only reiterate ancient economic verities and brood over affronts to his dignity. Rather than giving new ideologies a fair hearing, he found them evil and insidious. For a man of such delicacy, who reported late to work so he could watch the tulips bloom, he could be heartless with his supposed enemies—Jews, Catholics, Germans, liberals, reformers, and intellectuals—whom he lumped together into a single nefarious plot. “The world knew him only as a somewhat mysterious colossus of finance,” said The *New York Herald Tribune.*[10](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2379595) If the world saw remarkably little of his compassion, he had himself to blame for it. He never gave of himself freely to the public. At bottom, he didn’t believe in common humanity and imagined his foes driven by motives unlike his own. Instead of accepting change as a fact of life, he raged against his moment in history and suffered in the process.
在一个亟需新思维的时代，杰克却只能翻来覆去地念叨那些陈腐的经济真理，为自尊受到的冒犯而耿耿于怀。他不愿公正地看待新兴思想，反而视其为邪恶阴险之物。像他这样细腻的人——甚至会为了看郁金香盛开而迟到上班——却对所谓的敌人冷酷无情：犹太人、天主教徒、德国人、自由派、改革者、知识分子，他把他们统统归入一个险恶的阴谋中。“世人只知他是个有些神秘的金融巨擘，”《纽约先驱论坛报》如是说。 [10](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2379595) 世人若少有见到他的恻隐之心，那也只能怪他自己。他从未向公众敞开心扉。骨子里，他不相信普通人性，认为自己的对手都怀有别样的动机。他不愿接受变化是生活的一部分，反而对时代洪流愤恨不已，最终自己也深受其苦。

That Jack Morgan was an anachronism could be seen by the fate of his possessions: only institutions could afford his boats and residences. *Corsair IV* was bought by Pacific Cruise Lines and converted into a cruise ship for eighty-five passengers. His Georgian brick mansion on Long Island was rented to the Soviet UN delegation in 1949. Soviet diplomats and their families played volleyball on a lawn that once had been owned by the czar’s banker; in the mansion, they installed seventy-one beds, sixty-seven canvas chairs, and eight big cafeteria tables. The town of Glen Cove objected to this use of the property, and the Russians had to depart. For many years afterward, the estate served as a convent for the Sisters of Saint John the Baptist, who built a chapel in the courtyard between the main house and Jack’s sixteen-car garage. The mansion was later torn down, and one hundred suburban houses were put up on the old estate grounds. The fifteen-hundred-acre Camp Uncas in the Adirondacks was bought by the Boy Scouts, while the United Lutheran Church paid a scant \$245,000 in 1949 for Jack’s forty-five-room Madison Avenue townhouse. In 1988, when the Lutherans decamped for Chicago, they sold it back to the Pierpont Morgan Library for \$15 million. Wall Hall was acquired by the county council for a green belt around London. Princes Gate, once among the finest private homes in London, became headquarters of the Independent Television Authority in the 1950s (and in 1980 had as its neighbor just a few doors away the Iranian embassy, which in that year was the scene of a violent siege). The world of the grandees was over. In the post-World War II era the Wall Street and City banks would grow into vast, global institutions of a hitherto unimaginable size. But the bankers inhabiting them would, paradoxically, seem that much smaller.
杰克·摩根之落伍，观其财产归宿便知：唯有机构才买得起他的游艇与宅邸。Corsair IV 号被太平洋邮轮公司购入，改装成可载 85 名乘客的观光船。他在长岛的乔治亚式砖砌豪宅，1949 年租给了苏联驻联合国代表团。苏联外交官及其家属曾在曾属于沙皇银行家的草坪上打排球；宅内他们放置了 71 张床、67 把帆布椅和八张大食堂餐桌。格伦科夫镇对此用途提出异议，俄国人只得离去。此后多年，该庄园成为圣若翰洗者修女会的修道院，修女们在主楼与杰克的十六车位车库之间的庭院里建了一座小教堂。后来豪宅被拆除，旧庄园的土地上建起了百栋郊区住宅。 阿迪朗达克山脉中占地一千五百英亩的安卡斯营地被童子军买下，而美国路德宗会则在 1949 年仅以 24.5 万美元购得杰克位于麦迪逊大道的四十五个房间的联排别墅。1988 年，路德宗会迁往芝加哥后，以 1500 万美元将其卖回给皮尔庞特·摩根图书馆。沃尔庄园被郡议会收购，用作伦敦周边的绿化带。普林斯门曾是伦敦最豪华的私人住宅之一，在 20 世纪 50 年代成为独立电视管理局总部（并在 1980 年与伊朗大使馆仅隔几个门，同年那里发生了一场暴力围困事件）。权贵们的世界就此终结。二战后的时代，华尔街和伦敦金融城的银行发展成规模空前的庞大全球性机构。但矛盾的是，身处其中的银行家们却显得渺小了许多。

FOR central bankers of the Diplomatic Age, the war proved a time of melancholy reflection. Monty Norman bemoaned the curse of modern democracy, of making decisions by “counting noses,” as he scornfully phrased it. He blamed politicians for destroying the rational system of gold-linked currencies that he and his Morgan friends had created in the 1920s. All had foundered on the rocks of nationalism and politics. Finance, it turned out, wasn’t a sterile laboratory that could be run by scientific bankers in white coats. Nor could it be left to a mysterious, self-appointed priesthood. In the Casino Age, central and private banks would no longer function as sovereign states but would be linked to government entities, both national and multilateral.
对身处于外交时代的央行行长们来说，这场战争无疑是一段令人黯然神伤的反思期。蒙蒂·诺曼哀叹现代民主的诅咒——他嘲讽地称之为“靠数人头做决策”。他指责政客们毁掉了 1920 年代他与其摩根朋友们共同构建的理性金本位货币体系。这一切最终都撞碎在民族主义和政治的暗礁之上。事实证明，金融并非一个无菌实验室，不能由穿白大褂的科学家式银行家来掌控，也无法交由神秘的自封祭司阶层来打理。在赌场时代，央行和私人银行不再作为主权国家运作，而是将与国内外政府实体紧密相连。

Throughout the war, Russell Leffingwell sent food packages to Monty Norman. A rattled man wanting reassurance, Norman asked Leffingwell whether he was wrong about the gold standard and his attempts to refurbish the old imperial pound. Any other course of action, Norman pleaded, would “have shaken the confidence in Europe, and have produced a feeling of uncertainty, which seemed the one thing to be avoided.”[11](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2379746) Leffingwell agreed that only gold formed a bulwark against the modern plagues of managed currencies, budget deficits, and bloated welfare states. He, too, recognized the futility of their labors: “How we labored together, you and Ben, my partners and I, to rebuild the world after the last war—and look at the d——thing now!”[12](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2379942) Monty was equally despondent: “As I look back, it now seems that, with all the thought and work and good intentions which we provided, we achieved *absolutely* nothing. . . . I think we should have done just as much good if we had been able to collect the money and pour it down the drain.”[13](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2380109)
战争期间，拉塞尔·莱芬韦尔（Russell Leffingwell）一直给蒙蒂·诺曼（Monty Norman）寄送食品包裹。诺曼心神不宁，渴望得到 reassurance，便问莱芬韦尔，自己对金本位以及重振旧日英镑的种种努力是否错了。他恳求道，任何其他做法都“会动摇欧洲的信心，引发不安情绪，而这恰恰是应该避免的。” [11](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2379746) 莱芬韦尔也同意，只有黄金才能筑起一道防线，抵御现代种种困境——管理货币、预算赤字、臃肿的福利国家。他也同样意识到自己的努力徒劳无功：“我们曾多么齐心协力啊，你和本，还有我和我的合伙人，一起在上次战后重建世界——可看看现在这鬼样子！” [12](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2379942) 蒙蒂同样沮丧：“回首往事，现在看来，尽管我们倾注了那么多心血、辛劳和善意，却一事无成……” 我认为，即便我们当时真能把钱收上来然后倒进下水道，效果恐怕也差不多。 [13](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2380109)

There would be a day of reckoning for Monty Norman no less than there had been for Ben Strong. The Labour party had never forgiven him for his tough attitude toward the first Labour government in the 1920s, nor for the austerity imposed in 1925 on behalf of the gold standard. When the government abandoned gold in 1931, it only reinforced suspicions that financial “rules” were ruses to intimidate recalcitrant left-wing governments. The bitter 1931 gibe of Labour party veteran Beatrice Webb—“Nobody told us we could do it”—still reverberated. Now Norman’s autocratic twenty-four year reign at the Bank of England would belatedly produce new government controls over British finance.
蒙蒂·诺曼终将迎来清算之日，正如本·斯特朗当年所经历的那样。工党从未原谅他在 20 世纪 20 年代对首届工党政府所采取的强硬态度，也未曾忘记 1925 年他为维护金本位制而推行的紧缩政策。1931 年英国政府放弃金本位后，这更让工党坚信：所谓金融"规则"，不过是用来恫吓不听话的左翼政府的伎俩。工党元老比阿特丽斯·韦布在 1931 年那句苦涩的讥讽——"没人告诉我们还能这么干"——至今仍余音绕梁。如今，诺曼在英格兰银行长达二十四年的独裁统治，终于促使政府对英国金融体系实施了新的管控。

Norman’s health declined in 1943 and 1944 and he was diagnosed as having pneumonia and then meningitis. Fragile and broken in his seventies, he heeded doctors’ advice that he resign. For several years, Tom Catto had been mentioned as a successor, and his conscientious work at the wartime Treasury impressed Norman. Although Catto had been the sole liberal at the solidly Tory Morgan Grenfell, the Labour party feared he would perpetuate City rule at the Bank of England. As early as 1940, Hugh Dalton, minister of economic warfare, warned Chancellor of the Exchequer Kingsley Wood that “there will be much feeling against Catto as successor. He comes from the most reactionary firm in the City, Morgan Grenfell, who, I say, have a notorious record as partisan Tories.”[14](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2380269)
1943 年至 1944 年间，诺曼的健康状况每况愈下，先后被诊断出肺炎和脑膜炎。年逾七旬、身体孱弱的他听从医生建议选择辞职。多年来，汤姆·卡托一直被视作继任人选，他在战时财政部勤勉尽责的工作给诺曼留下了深刻印象。尽管卡托是铁杆保守党阵营摩根建富中唯一的自由派，但工党担心他会延续伦敦金融城对英格兰银行的统治。早在 1940 年，经济战争大臣休·道尔顿就警告财政大臣金斯利·伍德：“卡托作为继任者会引发强烈反对。他来自金融城最反动的公司摩根建富，这家公司作为顽固保守党的名声可谓臭名昭著。” [14](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2380269)

Chosen governor in 1944, Catto made a wistful pilgrimage to Norman’s country house to receive the older man’s blessing. “My dear, Catto,” Norman said, “I had been my own first choice for re-election as Governor of the Bank of England, but the doctors say ’No.’ You are my second choice. God bless you.”[15](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2380437) Touched by this gesture, Catto broke down and paced the garden with Norman’s wife before he could regain his composure. Catto’s appointment was interpreted as underscoring the need for close postwar cooperation with the United States.
1944 年，卡托被选为行长，他心怀感慨地前往诺曼的乡间别墅，以求得这位前辈的祝福。“亲爱的卡托，”诺曼说道，“我原本自己是连任英格兰银行行长的第一人选，但医生们说‘不行’。你是我的第二选择。愿上帝保佑你。” [15](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2380437) 这番举动令卡托深受感动，他情绪失控，与诺曼的妻子在花园里踱步良久，才重新镇定下来。卡托的任命被解读为强调了战后与美国密切合作的必要性。

After the surprise defeat of Churchill’s government in the 1945 elections, Clement Attlee’s Labour government put nationalization of the bank at the top of its parliamentary agenda. Although the bank had long handled national debt, currency issues, and foreign exchange, it was privately owned by seventeen thousand stockholders. Now the central bankers were to be driven from the shadows in which they had operated. For Labour partisans, it was a long overdue act of revenge against Norman.
在 1945 年大选中丘吉尔政府意外落败后，克莱门特·艾德礼领导的工党政府将银行国有化列为议会首要议程。尽管该银行长期负责国债、货币发行及外汇事务，但其实际控制权却掌握在一万七千名私人股东手中。如今，这些央行银行家们将被驱逐出他们长期运作的阴影。对于工党支持者而言，这无疑是对诺曼迟来已久的报复。

Die-hards in the City thought Catto should resign as a matter of honor rather than supervise a bank under government control. Monty Norman never quite overcame the feeling that Catto should have been wily enough to defeat nationalization. Catto, in fact, proved a perfect transitional figure and probably secured a better deal for the bank than Norman could have. He wasn’t regarded as a City figure hostile to Whitehall and was a shrewd, conciliatory man. He recognized that Norman’s dictum, “Never excuse, never explain,” wouldn’t suffice in the new age. Central banks could no longer be priestly or hermetic, and Catto thought it best for a sound banker to manage the transition. To preserve the bank’s independence, he won agreement that the governor be appointed for five-year renewable terms and dismissed only by act of Parliament.
伦敦金融城的顽固派认为，卡托应当为了颜面主动辞职，而非去监管一家受政府控制的银行。蒙蒂·诺曼始终难以释怀，觉得卡托本该老谋深算地挫败国有化。事实上，卡托证明了自己是绝佳的过渡人物，为银行争取到的条件很可能比诺曼能做到的更好。他不被视为敌视白厅的金融城权贵，而是一位精明练达、善于斡旋的人。他深知诺曼那句"从不道歉，从不解释"的信条在新时代已行不通。中央银行不能再像祭司般神秘莫测，卡托认为由一位稳健的银行家来管理过渡最为妥当。为保全银行的独立性，他成功争取到行长任期五年并可连任，且只有议会法案才能将其免职。

In March 1946, after more than 250 years of independence, the Bank of England became a public institution. It would now be less influenced by merchant bankers, and more industrialists and union leaders would be appointed as its postwar directors. Catto told Lamont with relief, “The ship had to be steered between Scylla and Charybdis but we managed somehow!”[16](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2380597) After serving as governor until his seventieth birthday, Catto in 1949 took a desk again at Morgan Grenfell but didn’t resume formal duties at the firm. His son Stephen, however, would be a postwar Morgan Grenfell chairman.
1946 年 3 月，在独立经营超过 250 年后，英格兰银行成为一家公共机构。从此，它受商业银行家的影响会减小，更多实业家和工会领袖将被任命为战后董事。卡托如释重负地对拉蒙特说：“这艘船不得不在斯库拉和卡律布狄斯之间航行，但我们总算是挺过来了！” [16](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2380597) 卡托在总督任上一直干到七十岁生日，1949 年他回到摩根建富的办公桌前，但未再承担公司的正式职责。不过，他的儿子斯蒂芬后来成为了战后摩根建富的董事会主席。

For Monty Norman, the new world embodied everything he despised. Lamenting the “socialisation at a gallop” overtaking England, he told Leffingwell that he seldom went into the City anymore and found it a sad place, reduced to refunding bonds at lower interest rates. The man who had devoted his life to maintaining London as a world financial center now saw it as a place of faded glory: “I fear that the various ancient businesses of London have practically come to an end, or continue perhaps as shadows.”[17](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2380798) As foreign business shifted more decisively to New York than it had after World War I, leaving little room for London leadership, Norman seemed lost, unhinged, distraught. “I wonder what old Ben would think of all this,” he said.[18](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2381011) On February 4, 1950, Montagu Norman died, having suffered a stroke the year before.
对于蒙塔古·诺曼而言，这个新世界恰恰是他所憎恶的一切。他哀叹着席卷英国的“飞速社会化”，并告诉莱芬韦尔，自己已很少再踏足伦敦金融城，只觉得那里令人伤感，沦为了以更低利率偿还债券的地方。这位毕生致力于维持伦敦世界金融中心地位的人，如今看到的却是一派辉煌褪去的景象：“恐怕伦敦那些古老的行当已几乎走到尽头，即使勉强延续，也不过是苟延残喘的影子罢了。” [17](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2380798) 随着海外业务比一战后更为决绝地转向纽约，伦敦的领导地位已所剩无几，诺曼仿佛迷失了方向，精神恍惚，悲痛欲绝。“不知道老本看到这一切会作何感想。”他说道。 [18](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2381011) 1950 年 2 月 4 日，蒙塔古·诺曼因前一年中风发作，与世长辞。

AFTER the war, a hearty survivor, Dr. Hjalmar Horace Greeley Schacht, had resumed his correspondence with Monty Norman. Arrested by the Gestapo in July 1944 after participating in another plot against Hitler, he was sent to the Ravensbrueck concentration camp and ultimately passed through thirty-two prisons, including the death camp at Dachau. He formed part of a distinguished group of prisoners that included the former Austrian chancellor Kurt von Schuschnigg and Leon Blum. In the last stages of war, their captors hurried them southeast to escape the advancing American troops. On May 4, 1945, Schacht and the others were about to be executed by the Gestapo, under express orders from Hitler, when they were liberated by Allied troops in the south Tirol.
战后，一位意志坚韧的幸存者——沙赫特博士，重新恢复了与蒙蒂·诺曼的书信往来。他因参与另一起反希特勒密谋于 1944 年 7 月被盖世太保逮捕，被送往拉文斯布吕克集中营，最终辗转三十二座监狱，其中包括达豪死亡集中营。他与一批显赫的囚犯关押在一起，其中有前奥地利总理库尔特·冯·许士尼格和莱昂·布鲁姆。战争末期，看守者带着他们向东南方向仓皇转移，以躲避推进中的美军部队。1945 年 5 月 4 日，沙赫特和其他囚犯即将在希特勒的直接命令下被盖世太保处决之际，盟军在南蒂罗尔将他们解救了出来。

Schacht tried to visit the ailing Norman, but he hadn’t been officially de-Nazified and was denied an English visa. He was a shameless, bull-headed man whose sheer arrogance seemed to preserve him in adversity. After being indicted as a war criminal by the Nuremberg tribunal, he was placed under arrest by General Lucius Clay, commander of the U.S. Army of Occupation in Germany. When Clay went to Schacht’s chalet outside Berlin to take him into custody, Schacht resisted the notion that he was anti-American. As proof, he told Clay, “Look at the picture on the wall.” It was a signed photograph that David Sarnoff had given him at the Young Plan conference in Paris in 1929.[19](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2381185)
沙赫特曾试图探望病中的诺曼，但因尚未正式完成去纳粹化程序，被英国拒签。他是个厚颜无耻、固执己见的家伙，那股傲气反倒让他在逆境中屹立不倒。纽伦堡法庭以战犯罪名起诉他后，美国驻德占领军司令卢修斯·克莱将军将其逮捕。当克莱前往沙赫特位于柏林郊外的别墅拘捕他时，沙赫特坚称自己绝非反美分子，并指着墙上的照片说：“看那幅画。”那是 1929 年巴黎杨格计划会议上，戴维·萨尔诺夫送给他的签名照。

Awaiting the Nuremberg war trials at a prison camp, Schacht continued to behave in a bizarrely unpredictable manner. Albert Speer, Hitler’s architect and minister of armaments, recalled how Schacht delivered dramatic poetry readings to pass the time. When American military psychologists delivered IQ tests to the war criminals, Schacht scored first in the group. There were many strange reunions at Nuremberg. Schacht hadn’t seen Hermann Göring since losing out in the power struggle to him in 1937. “Our next meeting was in prison at Nuremberg when we were taken to a cell with 2 bathtubs where—I in one and Goering in the other—we each proceeded to soap ourselves all over,” Schacht wrote. “Sic transit gloria mundi!”[20](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2381354)
在监狱营地等待纽伦堡战争审判期间，沙赫特依旧表现得难以捉摸、出人意料。希特勒的建筑师兼军备部长阿尔伯特·施佩尔回忆，沙赫特靠朗诵戏剧诗作打发时间。美国军事心理学家对这批战犯进行智商测试时，沙赫特在群体中得分最高。纽伦堡上演了许多离奇的重逢。自从 1937 年在权力斗争中输给赫尔曼·戈林后，沙赫特便再未见过他。“我们下次见面是在纽伦堡的监狱里，当时我们被带进一间放有两个浴缸的牢房——我躺在一个浴缸里，戈林躺在另一个——各自开始往身上涂肥皂，”沙赫特写道。“世间的荣耀就这样烟消云散了！”

At Nuremberg, Schacht refused to admit responsibility for Hitler’s success and denied he had made a unique contribution to the Nazi cause. He said of Hitler, “He would have found other methods and other assistance; he was not the man to give up.”[21](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2381531) Schacht could document enough resistance in the late 1930s to offset the impression of collaboration with the Nazis. He cast himself as a solitary critic of the regime who was appalled by the cowardice of workers, liberals, churchmen, and scientists. So the man who rallied Krupp, Thyssen, and other German industrialists around Adolf Hitler and helped mold the robust German war economy was one of only three Nazis acquitted at Nuremberg. A German de-Nazification court afterward convicted him as a major Nazi offender, and he was sentenced to eight years in a labor camp, although he appealed and was released after a year. In the 1950s, he wrote a long-winded, self-adoring autobiography that was conspicuously short on contrition for his role in Nazi finance. He died unrepentant in 1970, at the age of ninety-three, of complications resulting from a fall.
在纽伦堡审判中，沙赫特拒绝承认自己对希特勒的成功负有责任，也否认曾为纳粹事业做出独特贡献。谈及希特勒时，他说：“他总能找到其他方法和帮手——他绝不是轻言放弃的人。” [21](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2381531) 沙赫特能拿出足够证据，证明自己在 1930 年代末曾进行过抵抗，从而冲淡了他与纳粹合作的印象。他将自己塑造成体制的孤独批评者，对工人、自由派、神职人员和科学家的懦弱感到震惊。就这样，那个曾把克虏伯、蒂森等德国工业巨头聚拢在阿道夫·希特勒身边、并助力打造了强大德国战争经济的人，竟成了纽伦堡审判中仅有的三名被无罪释放的纳粹分子之一。后来，德国的一个去纳粹化法庭判定他为纳粹重犯，判处八年劳改营监禁，但他上诉后仅服刑一年便获释。 上世纪 50 年代，他写了一本冗长且自恋的自传，但书中对自己在纳粹金融体系中所扮演的角色鲜有悔意。 1970 年，他至死无悔，终年 93 岁，死因是跌倒引发的并发症。

STARTING in 1943, Tom Lamont had heart trouble and no longer reported regularly to the bank. Toward the end of the war, his handsome grandson Thomas W. Lamont II, died aboard the submarine *Snook* in the Pacific. Now in his seventies and nostalgic with age, Lamont composed a charming volume of memoirs about his boyhood in a country parsonage. His romantic nature never flagged. Throughout the war, he had sent food parcels to Nancy Astor, who even in her sixties was energetic enough to perform cartwheels in the air-raid shelters. In 1945, after she had retired from twenty-five years in Parliament, Lamont paid \$3,000 in expenses for her to visit the United States. On the eve of her visit, he wrote her, “And meantime with your war cares largely removed I shall find you I know looking younger and lovelier than ever before.” Then he added, forgetfully, “How proud you must be that it was Britain who in 1940 stood all alone against the entire world of barbarism and saved civilization.”[22](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2381666)
自 1943 年起，汤姆·拉蒙特便因心脏问题不再按时去银行上班。战争临近尾声时，他那位英俊的孙子小托马斯·W·拉蒙特在太平洋海域的“斯努克号”潜艇上牺牲。如今已年过七旬、愈发怀旧的拉蒙特，写下了一本温馨的回忆录，讲述他在乡间牧师宅邸度过的童年时光。他浪漫的天性从未消退。战争期间，他一直给南希·阿斯特寄送食物包裹，即便已年过六旬，她依然精力充沛，能在防空洞里翻筋斗。1945 年，在她结束长达二十五年的议员生涯后，拉蒙特出资三千美元资助她访美。在她启程前夕，他写信道：“如今战争的重担已卸去大半，我相信再见你时，你定会比以往更显年轻动人。”随即他又不无感慨地补充道：“你一定倍感自豪吧——1940 年，是英国独自挺身而出，对抗整个野蛮世界，拯救了文明。”

Unpleasant reminders of the past would intrude. In 1944, the Italian government dispatched a financial mission to Lamont. Some of the old gang wanted to crank up the Italy-America Society, but Lamont suggested that maybe they should wait awhile. When news came of Mussolini’s death in 1945, Lamont said its “indecent” manner upset him but that otherwise nobody regretted it. With a new anti-Fascist mood in postwar Italy, Lamont took pains to rewrite history. In 1946, he told Count Giuseppe Volpi, the former finance minister, that the \$100-million loan to Italy in 1926 was made under duress. He implied that he had frowned upon it: “I hardly have to say that the loan was not one that we were eager to arrange, nor was it sought by us. On the contrary, it was a part of the series of post-war reconstruction operations encouraged by our own Government.”[23](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2381851)
过去那些不愉快的回忆总会不期而至。1944 年，意大利政府曾向拉蒙特派遣一个金融使团。一些旧派人物想要重启意大利-美国协会，但拉蒙特建议他们不妨再等等。1945 年墨索里尼死讯传来时，拉蒙特表示其“不体面”的死法让他不安，但除此之外无人为之惋惜。战后意大利兴起新的反法西斯情绪，拉蒙特便费尽心机改写历史。1946 年，他对前财政部长朱塞佩·沃尔皮伯爵说，1926 年那笔 1 亿美元贷款其实是迫不得已之举。他暗示自己当时并不赞同：“我几乎不必明说，那笔贷款并非我们急于操办，也不是我们主动寻求的。恰恰相反，它是我国政府所鼓励的一系列战后重建行动中的一环。” [23](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2381851)

By war’s end, Lamont came to the bank only for short periods each week. He continued to make the grandly liberal gestures that had marked his extraordinary tenure at 23 Wall. For \$2 million, he endowed an undergraduate library at Harvard—appropriately, it would be for government documents—and sent what Norman called a “whomping” check for restoring Canterbury Cathedral. He ended his banking career with a Pierpontian act of munificence: in the lean year of 1947, the firm had skipped bonus payments; Lamont decided to compensate by giving every staff member a Christmas gift equal to 5 percent of his or her salary.
战争结束时，拉蒙特每周只在银行待很短时间。他继续做出那些标志着他华尔街 23 号非凡任期的慷慨大度之举。他出资 200 万美元为哈佛大学捐建了一座本科生图书馆——恰如其分地，它将用于存放政府文献——并向诺曼所谓的"巨额的"支票用于修复坎特伯雷大教堂。他以一个皮尔庞特式的慷慨之举结束了自己的银行生涯：在 1947 年这个拮据的年份，公司没有发放奖金；拉蒙特决定补偿这一点，给每位员工发放相当于其工资 5%的圣诞礼物。

Lamont had time to wonder about the hopefulness that had buoyed him between the wars, making him susceptible to the false allure of appeasement. He now saw Americans as too self-absorbed by materialism and too coddled by peace to brace for violence. In a 1945 essay, “Germany’s Heartbreak House,” he tried to figure out why the Allies were deaf to Churchill’s pleas regarding Hitler. He wrote:
拉蒙特不禁思考，是什么让他在两次战争之间抱有希望，以至于轻易被绥靖政策的虚假诱惑所蒙蔽。如今在他看来，美国人过于沉迷物质享受，又被和平环境宠坏，无法面对暴力。在 1945 年的一篇题为《德国的伤心之家》的文章中，他试图弄明白为什么盟国对丘吉尔关于希特勒的警告充耳不闻。他写道：

> The truth is that the British and French, like us Americans, are so peace-loving that it has always been hard for them to realize that there are gangster peoples going about the world seeking whom they may devour. We have all refused to believe until the last moment that there were Dillinger nations prowling about with completely laid plans of evil portent. . . . For in the makeup of the Anglo-Saxon peoples there is . . . that extreme of humaneness that abhors cruelty and will have naught of it.[24](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2382034)
> 事实是，英国人和法国人，就像我们美国人一样，太热爱和平了，以至于他们一直很难意识到，这个世界上存在着一些四处游荡、寻找吞噬目标的强盗民族。我们都拒绝相信，直到最后一刻才明白，原来有一些如同迪林杰一般的国家，早已策划好了充满邪恶预兆的阴谋，四处横行……因为盎格鲁-撒克逊民族的性格中……有一种极端的人道主义，它厌恶残酷，并拒绝接受任何残酷之事。 [24](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2382034)

The explanation omits the large measure of self-interest that had led Lamont to cling first to Japan, then to Italy.
这一解释忽略了一个重要因素：拉蒙特当初先是紧抱日本不放，后又死缠意大利，这其中很大程度上是出于自身利益。

On February 3, 1948, Tom Lamont died at his home in Boca Grande, Florida, at age seventy-seven, and Russell Leffingwell became chairman of the House of Morgan. So many friends flocked to Lamont’s funeral at the Brick Presbyterian Church on Park Avenue that folding chairs were hastily arranged in the side aisles and balcony. Two veterans of Black Thursday—William Potter of Guaranty Trust and Albert Wiggin of Chase—were in evidence. Whereas at Jack’s funeral the mourners sang “Onward Christian Soldiers,” at Tom’s passages from Milton’s *Samson Agonistes* were read against a brilliant backdrop of white flowers.
1948 年 2 月 3 日，汤姆·拉蒙特在佛罗里达州博卡格兰德的家中去世，享年七十七岁，拉塞尔·莱芬韦尔接任摩根银行董事长。拉蒙特的葬礼在公园大道的砖砌长老会教堂举行，前来吊唁的人络绎不绝，教堂侧廊和楼座紧急摆满了折叠椅。两位“黑色星期四”的亲历者——担保信托公司的威廉·波特和大通银行的阿尔伯特·威金——赫然在列。如果说杰克的葬礼上，人们高唱的是《基督精兵前进》，那么在汤姆的葬礼上，则是在灿烂的白花映衬下，朗读了弥尔顿《力士参孙》的片段。

Lamont’s estate was so enormous that the charitable and educational bequests came to \$9.5 million, including \$5 million to Harvard and \$2 million to Phillips Exeter Academy. Through a syndicate managed by Morgan Stanley, his estate sold off his twenty-five-thousand-share block of J. P. Morgan stock. It was the largest block in existence, with an estimated market value of nearly \$6 million.
拉蒙特的遗产规模惊人，用于慈善和教育的遗赠高达 950 万美元，其中哈佛大学获得 500 万，菲利普斯埃克塞特学院获得 200 万。通过摩根士丹利牵头的一个财团，他的遗产将手中持有的 2.5 万股 J.P.摩根股票悉数出售——这是市场上最大的一批流通股，当时估值近 600 万美元。

Lamont was a man of prodigious gifts, the real J. P. Morgan behind J. P. Morgan and Company. Had he lived in Pierpont’s day, he might have summoned steel mills or transcontinental railroads into being. Instead, as a man of the Diplomatic Age, he was the architect of huge state loans in the 1920s. As they defaulted in the 1930s, he had to devote his time to fruitless salvage operations, and his gifts were squandered in the general wreckage. For all his power, he seems in retrospect a tiny figure bobbing atop a gigantic tidal wave. His story is a sobering tale of human limitations.
拉蒙特天赋异禀，堪称 J.P.摩根公司背后真正的“摩根”。若他生逢皮尔庞特的时代，或许能缔造钢铁帝国或横贯大陆的铁路网。然而生为“外交时代”的弄潮儿，他成了 20 世纪 20 年代巨额国家贷款的擘画者。到了 30 年代这些贷款相继违约，他不得不投身于徒劳无果的救赎行动，最终在满盘皆输中才情尽废。纵使权倾一时，如今回望，他也不过是惊涛骇浪上浮沉的一粒微尘。他的故事，是对人类局限性的清醒昭示。

In its front-page obituary, the *Times* said that the driving force of Lamont’s life “was an unremitting search for the good, the full and the gracious life.”[25](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2382262) Indeed, one admires his ambition to lead a beautiful, rounded life and bring poetry into the straitlaced world of banking. He gave a literary gloss and an intellectual richness to the House of Morgan, stretching the sense of what it means to be a banker. He was a man who dealt with the large issues of the day, saw the strategic significance of his actions, and transcended a provincial concern with profit. His vision of banking was remarkably spacious.
《泰晤士报》在其头版讣告中指出，拉蒙特一生的动力源于"对美好、圆满与优雅生活的不懈追求" 。 [25](#The_House_of_Morgan_split_049.html_filepos2382262) 的确，他渴望过上美好而丰盈的生活，并将诗歌引入刻板的银行业，这种志向令人钦佩。他为摩根财团注入了文学的光彩和思想的深度，拓展了银行家这一身份的内涵。他处理时代重大议题，洞察自身行动的深远意义，超越了狭隘的逐利视角。他对银行业的构想，格局非凡。

Yet he resorted to moral shortcuts and political compromises. He was too quick to paper over conflicts with rhetoric and to settle arguments with smiles. The optimism that made him an inspiring leader also contained an element of pure opportunism. He refused to terminate business relationships until *force majeure* supervened, and his complicity with Japanese militarists and Mussolini are black marks on his record. By the end, he could no longer distinguish policy from public relations or separate means from ends. In trying to please too many people, he lost the habit of truth—a habit, once lost, that can never be regained. Perhaps the most extraordinary figure in Morgan history, Lamont was a dreamer whose reach exceeded his grasp. He fell short of the ideals that he himself articulated. After his death, Wall Street would seem grayer and more bureaucratic. As a confidant of presidents, prime ministers, and kings, he was the last great banker of the Diplomatic Age.
但他还是选择了道德捷径和政治妥协。他过于轻率地用辞藻掩饰矛盾，用笑容平息争论。那份使他成为鼓舞人心领袖的乐观主义中，也夹杂着纯粹的机会主义成分。直到不可抗力降临，他才终止商业关系；他与日本军国主义者及墨索里尼的合谋，成为其履历上的污点。到最后，他已分不清政策与公关，辨不明手段与目的。为取悦太多人，他失去了说真话的习惯——这习惯一旦失去，便永远无法找回。作为摩根家族史上最非凡的人物之一，拉蒙特是个心比天高的梦想家。他未能企及自己阐述的理想。他去世后，华尔街显得更加灰暗、更加官僚。作为总统、首相和国王的密友，他是外交时代最后一位伟大的银行家。
